# Backtest Methodology for Rare-Event, Timing-Window, and Race Questions

Written 2026-07-14, ahead of France vs Spain (2026-07-14 semifinal), specifically to answer
whether Q4 (on-field VAR review), Q11 (goal between hydration breaks), and Q14 (Spain first
substitution) can be backed by a validated statistical model or backtest, or whether they sit
outside what this project's evidence base can support.

Scope note: this document does not re-survey the Dixon-Coles/Maher/bivariate-Poisson/
Constantinou-Fenton lineage, or the ML4T backtesting-discipline material (walk-forward,
purging/embargo, Deflated Sharpe Ratio, White's Reality Check, multiple-testing correction) —
all of that is already covered in `ML_MODEL_BUILD_RESEARCH_2026-07-13.md` (see especially
B.1, B.2, B.8, C.1-C.5) and is cited here, not repeated. This document goes past that survey on
two specific, previously-uncovered questions: (A) what the original football-modeling papers
actually say about validating rare sub-events specifically (not full-match results), and (B)
what quantitative-finance rare-event backtesting methodology (VaR exception testing,
survival-analysis validation, point-process model validation) has to say about whether a
model or heuristic for Q4/Q11/Q14-style questions can ever be honestly "backtested" at this
project's sample size.

Sourcing convention used throughout: **"Source:"** marks a claim read directly from the cited
paper/page (via web search snippet, abstract, or fetched page — noted per claim). **"Synthesis:"**
marks this document's own inference or transfer to the JTC context. Where a paper could not be

## Task A — What the original football-model papers say about out-of-sample validation

### A.1 Maher (1982), "Modelling association football scores," *Statistica Neerlandica* 36(3):109-118

Full text obtained and read directly (PDF mirror at http://www.90minut.pl/misc/maher.pdf; the paper is not open-access at the publisher).

**Source, data and fitting.** Maher used twelve separate season-division datasets from the Rothmans Football Yearbook (1973-75): the four English Football League divisions for each of the 1971-72, 1972-73 and 1973-74 seasons. Maximum-likelihood attack/defence/home-advantage parameters were fitted per data set via alternating iteration (an EM-like scheme).

**Source, validation methodology.** Maher's validation is chi-square goodness-of-fit on the *same data the model was fitted to* — there is no chronological train/test split and no holdout period anywhere in the paper. For each of the twelve data sets, the fitted attack/defence parameters were used to generate expected home- and away-score frequency distributions, compared against the observed frequencies with a chi-square statistic (Table 4/5 in the paper). Two of twelve home-score tests and three of twelve away-score tests were significant at the 5% level; Maher's own summary: "the independent Poisson model gives a reasonably accurate description of football scores... although with some slight doubt." A parallel chi-square test was run on the goal-difference distribution (independent-Poisson vs. a bivariate-Poisson with correlation ρ≈0.2), where four of twelve tests were significant for the independent model but the bivariate correction with ρ=0.2 fixed the fit in eleven of twelve.

**Source, the power-relevant finding.** The paper contains one directly quotable, load-bearing passage for this document's purpose: "The differences are small and over just one season do not seriously inflate the χ² value, but if the observed and expected frequencies for all twelve seasons are added the values of the χ² statistics (16.2 and 28.8 for home and away scores respectively) would lead to clear rejection of the model." (p.115-116). In other words: at the sample size of one division-season (~250 matches → ~460-500 team-score observations), Maher's own test could not reliably distinguish "independent Poisson is exactly right" from "independent Poisson has a small but real systematic bias" — the bias was only detectable once twelve seasons' worth of observations were pooled. This is Maher's own data demonstrating a statistical-power problem in-sample, for the *bulk* score distribution — a far higher-frequency target than any of Q4/Q11/Q14.

**What Maher does NOT do, explicitly.** No genuine out-of-sample/holdout test. No discussion of validating rare sub-events (single scorelines, specific minute-windows, or race-type events) separately from the aggregate score distribution — the paper's entire empirical content is the home/away goal-count distribution and the score-difference distribution, both aggregated across a full season. No discussion of minimum sample size as a named concept, though the twelve-vs-one-season pooling result is exactly that argument made implicitly.

### A.2 Dixon & Coles (1997), "Modelling Association Football Scores and Inefficiencies in the Football Betting Market," *JRSS Series C* 46(2):265-280

**Access note.** The publisher (Oxford Academic/Wiley for JRSS-C) paywalls the full text; multiple secondary hosts (academia.edu, Grokipedia) returned HTTP 403/blocked to automated fetch this session. What follows is built from the abstract (confirmed directly from the Oxford Academic and Lancaster/Bristol institutional-repository pages) plus multiple independent secondary summaries (a University of Bristol research-repository page, a Lancaster ePrints record, and several implementation write-ups (opisthokonta.net, Grokipedia) that cite specific fitted parameter values). **The following is source-confirmed at the abstract level; anything below marked "secondary" was not verified against the primary text.**

**Source (abstract, directly confirmed).** "A parametric model is developed and fitted to English league and cup football data from 1992 to 1995. The model is motivated by an aim to exploit potential inefficiencies in the association football betting market, and this is examined using bookmakers' odds from 1995 to 1996." This is a genuine chronological out-of-sample design: three seasons (1992-95) to fit, a distinct fourth season (1995-96) of bookmaker odds to test against — a real train/test split, unlike Maher's.

**Secondary (consistent across sources, not primary-text-verified).** The model's now-famous τ correction touches exactly four scorelines (0-0, 1-0, 0-1, 1-1) via a correlation parameter ρ, fitted on the same 1992-95 pseudo-likelihood as the rest of the model (commonly cited fitted value ρ≈-0.13 on English league data, per multiple independent secondary write-ups). The betting-strategy test showed a positive return over the 1995-96 season (secondary sources agree on direction; I could not verify an exact percentage return, stake count, or staking rule from the primary text this session — flagged as **not found**, not reconstructed).

**Critical gap, honestly flagged.** I could not access any passage — primary or secondary — describing a *dedicated, separate* validation of the low-score dependence correction (the τ/ρ mechanism) against held-out data, as distinct from its contribution to the model's aggregate log-likelihood or to overall betting profitability. The τ correction is exactly the part of Dixon-Coles closest in spirit to "rare/correlated sub-event modeling" (it exists specifically because 0-0/1-0/0-1/1-1 are a minority of scorelines where independent-Poisson visibly misfits), and if the original paper validated it with its own confidence interval or held-out chi-square test separate from the whole-model likelihood, that detail did not surface in any source reachable this session. Given the JRSS-C review process of the era, an aggregate in-sample likelihood-ratio test for the τ term is the more likely scenario (consistent with how Maher validated the analogous bivariate-Poisson correlation term, in-sample, A.1 above) — but this is **synthesis/inference, not a verified claim about what Dixon-Coles actually did.**

### A.3 Constantinou & Fenton — the "inadequate scoring rules" paper and the pi-ratings paper

Full text of both obtained directly (constantinou.info hosts open PDFs of both).

**Constantinou & Fenton (2012), "Solving the Problem of Inadequate Scoring Rules for Assessing Probabilistic Football Forecast Models," *JQAS* 8(1)** (http://constantinou.info/downloads/papers/solvingtheproblem.pdf). Source, read directly: the paper's entire scope is which scoring rule (RPS vs. Brier vs. others) is appropriate for the three-way, *ordinal* home-win/draw/away-win forecast — its argument is that W/D/L is a ranked scale and scoring rules that ignore ordinality (plain Brier, applied per-outcome) misprice "near misses" (predicting a narrow home win when a draw occurs) the same as "wild misses" (predicting a home win when it's an away win). I searched the full text directly for any treatment of low-base-rate outcomes, minimum sample size, or statistical power and found none — this paper does not address rare-event or small-sample forecast evaluation at all; its contribution is scoring-rule choice for a three-class, roughly-balanced-base-rate target. **Explicitly: searched, not found in this paper.** (This narrows, rather than contradicts, the project's own citation of it in `ML_MODEL_BUILD_RESEARCH_2026-07-13.md` B.2 — that citation is accurate to what the paper covers; it simply does not cover the rare-event question this document is asking.)

**Constantinou & Fenton (2013), pi-ratings paper, "Determining the level of ability of football teams by dynamic ratings based on the relative discrepancies in scores between adversaries," *JQAS* 9(1):37-50** (http://www.eecs.qmul.ac.uk/~norman/papers/pi-ratings.pdf, and a mirror at constantinou.info). Source, read directly:

- **Validation design.** A genuine chronological split: the first five EPL seasons (1992/93-1996/97) were used only to seed initial ratings (explicitly to avoid training the learning-rate parameters on uninformative "ignorant" starting ratings); the next ten seasons (1997/98-2006/07) were used to fit the two learning-rate hyperparameters (γ, γ*) by grid search minimizing squared goal-difference error; the final five seasons (2007/08-2011/12, 1900 match instances) were held out for the reported forecasting/profitability evaluation. This is a proper three-way chronological split (seed / train / test), a stronger design than either Maher or (as far as I could verify) Dixon-Coles' τ term specifically.
- **Source, an explicit small-sample design choice directly relevant to this project.** For the predictive distribution P(score diff | rating discrepancy), the paper states it discretized team-rating discrepancies into "28 levels... chosen to ensure that for any match combination... there are sufficient data points for a reasonably well informed prior." This is the pi-ratings paper doing, by hand, exactly the partial-pooling/binning judgment call this project's own hierarchical GLMM automates via `(1|team_name)` — coarsen the conditioning set until each cell has enough realizations to trust, and no finer. It is a directly transferable idea for Q4/Q11/Q14: if there is not enough data to condition a base rate on (e.g.) "SF-stage matches with Elo gap < 50," back off to a coarser cell ("all WC2026 knockout matches") until the cell is populous enough — never condition on a cell with a handful of observations just because it is topically closer.
- **Source, the profitability result and its own inherent power lesson.** The held-out five-season, 1900-match-instance betting simulation returned a net profit of £57.62 on £1887 staked (Table 1 in the paper) — a ~3.05% return on stakes across nearly two thousand match-outcome bets, with per-season results ranging from -£33.69 to +£60.16 (i.e., the model *lost* money in two of the five held-out seasons taken individually, and the pooled five-season result is what shows a positive edge). **Synthesis:** this is a useful real-world illustration of how much data even a well-designed, properly-out-of-sample-tested rating system needs before a genuine, still-fairly-modest edge on a *common* three-way event stabilizes to a confidently-positive sign — nearly 2,000 realizations, and even then the season-level result is noisy in both directions. Q4/Q11/Q14-style questions will not get within two orders of magnitude of that sample size this tournament.
- No discussion anywhere in the pi-ratings paper of rare/low-frequency sub-events, minimum sample size as a named statistical concept, or race/timing-window question types — the paper's target throughout is the three-way match outcome and the goal-difference regression.

### A.4 Baio & Blangiardo (2010), "Bayesian hierarchical model for the prediction of football results," *Journal of Applied Statistics* 37(2):253-264

Full text obtained directly (open PDF at UCL Discovery, https://discovery.ucl.ac.uk/16040/1/16040.pdf).

**Source, validation methodology — read directly, and this is the paper's central methodological choice for this document's purposes.** Baio & Blangiardo validate via **posterior predictive checking**, and they do it **in-sample**: for the Italian Serie A 1991-92 season (18 teams, one full season), they generate 1000 posterior-predictive replications of each team's season and compare the *observed* cumulative-points trajectory through the season against the model's *predicted* cumulative-points trajectory (Figure 2 in the paper), team by team, visually and via a league-table comparison. The same exercise is repeated for a second, independent single-season dataset (Serie A 2007-08, 20 teams, 360 matches) to demonstrate the overshrinkage problem and its mixture-model fix (Table 3, Figure 4) — but this is a *second in-sample posterior-predictive check on a different single season*, not a chronological holdout of the *same* fitted model. At no point in the paper is the model fit on one season and its forecasts scored against a *later, not-yet-observed* season.

**Synthesis — what this means for "does posterior predictive checking transfer to a rare-event question."** Posterior predictive checking is a legitimate and standard Bayesian model-criticism tool (compare replicated data under the fitted model to the actually-observed data), but as used in this specific paper it checks *aggregate season-level fit* (cumulative points, a smoothly-accumulating quantity built from ~34-38 matches per team) — not any single rare or low-frequency outcome. The technique itself would transfer to a rare-event question in principle (simulate many posterior-predictive replications of "did an on-field VAR review occur," check whether the observed count over N historical matches falls in a plausible region of the posterior-predictive distribution of counts) — but the *diagnostic power* of that check is bounded by exactly the same problem this whole document is about: with a handful of true historical on-field-review realizations, the posterior-predictive check has almost no ability to distinguish "my rare-event rate is right" from "my rare-event rate is off by 2x," because the predictive distribution of a small Poisson/Binomial count is wide relative to the plausible range of true rates. Baio & Blangiardo never test this regime; the transfer is this document's own inference, not a claim from the paper.

## Task B — Quant finance: backtesting rare-event / tail-probability forecasts

### B.1 Kupiec's Proportion of Failures (POF) test — methodology and its documented low-power problem

Source, read directly from a Federal Reserve Board working paper reviewing VaR backtesting (Campbell, "A Review of Backtesting and Backtesting Procedures," Fed Working Paper 2005-21, https://www.federalreserve.gov/pubs/feds/2005/200521/200521pap.pdf — full text obtained and read).

**The test.** Kupiec (1995), "Techniques for Verifying the Accuracy of Risk Management Models," *Journal of Derivatives*. Given T observations and I(α) = the count of VaR exceedances (days the realized loss exceeded the forecast VaR at confidence level α), the test statistic is the likelihood ratio

POF = 2·log[ (1-α̂)^(T-I(α)) · α̂^I(α) / ((1-α)^(T-I(α)) · α^I(α)) ],  α̂ = I(α)/T

which is asymptotically χ²(1) under the null that the true violation rate equals the nominal rate α. This is a direct structural analogue of Q4: "did the realized rate of a rare binary event (VaR exceedance / on-field VAR review) match the forecast probability," tested with a likelihood-ratio test on a count of successes out of T trials — exactly the same statistical object as "P(on-field review) = p, observed k reviews in n matches."

**Source, the concrete power numbers — the load-bearing finding of this whole section.** At the regulatory standard sample size (T=255 trading days, one year, 1% VaR), the paper reports: "Kupiec (1995) ... reports that when the threshold of 8 violations in one year is used in testing then the chance of detecting a VaR measure that reports the 3% VaR instead of the required 1% VaR ... is only 65%." That is: a model whose true tail-exceedance rate is *three times* the nominal rate (a severe, practically important miscalibration) is caught only 65% of the time — worse than the conventional 80% power target — using a full year of daily data. The paper continues: "the odds of detecting an institution that systematically under reports their VaR is slightly better than a coin flip" is the plain-language framing the paper itself uses for a *smaller* misspecification case elsewhere in the same discussion. Doubling the sample to two years raises detection power for the 3x-misstatement case to roughly 90% — "this increase in detection, however, comes at the cost of reducing the frequency with which the adequacy of the VaR measure can be assessed." The paper also flags, as a separate structural problem, that "the possibility that no violations occur in a period as short as one year is not trivial" — the POF statistic is literally undefined (log of zero) when zero exceedances are observed, which is the single most likely outcome for a rare event over a short observation window.

**Direct transfer to Q4.** Kupiec's own worked example uses T=255 (one trading year) as the baseline "regulatory" sample size and still finds only 65% power to catch a 3x miscalibration — and 255 is already roughly 2.5x this project's entire WC2026 match count (100 matches), and roughly the same order of magnitude as its combined WC2018+2022+2026 historical panel (256+ StatsBomb rows referenced in `PREREGISTRATION_sot_hierarchical_backtest.md` §"Training data"). A rare event with a much lower base rate than 1% VaR (on-field VAR reviews look to be on the order of 1-in-12 to 1-in-15 matches from this project's own hand-checked count, see §C below — call it 7-8%, actually a *higher* base rate than a 1% VaR exceedance) still needs many dozens of matches before a handful of realizations accumulate, and Kupiec's own math says even a full "regulatory year" of *daily* observations (255 i.i.d. Bernoulli trials) has middling power against severe misspecification. This project's total match count this tournament is roughly one-third that.

### B.2 Christoffersen's (1998) conditional coverage / independence test

Source, read directly from the same Fed working paper.

**The test and its purpose.** Christoffersen (1998), "Evaluating Interval Forecasts," *International Economic Review* 39. Kupiec's POF only checks the *unconditional* violation rate; it says nothing about whether violations cluster in time. Christoffersen's Markov test builds a 2×2 contingency table of consecutive-day exceedance pairs (violation-follows-violation vs. violation-follows-no-violation) and tests whether P(violation today | violation yesterday) = P(violation today | no violation yesterday); under the null these should be equal. The combined conditional-coverage statistic LR_CC = LR_UC (Kupiec) + LR_ind is asymptotically χ²(2). Source, directly quoted from the paper: "a streak of large unexpected losses may result in even more stress on a financial institution than large unexpected losses that occur ... spread out over time," and, critically for the regulatory framing, "while [8 clustered violations] would result [in] an increase in the institution's market risk capital multiplier, [4 consecutive violations] would not" under Kupiec alone — i.e., a model can pass Kupiec (correct average rate) while being dangerously wrong about *when* exceptions happen.

**Source, the independence test's own power problem — even more severe than Kupiec's.** The Fed paper states the Christoffersen-and-Pelletier (2004) duration-based extension exists specifically because the Markov test itself has low power ("Christoffersen and Pelletier (2004) provide some evidence that this test of independence has more power than the Markov test... in a setting in which the Markov test identifies an inaccurate VaR measure 28% of the time their duration based test identifies the inaccurate VaR measure 46% of the time" — i.e. even the *improved* test still misses more than half of genuinely miscalibrated models in their simulation). A second, independently-read source (value-at-risk.net's treatment of the same test, WebFetch summary this session) states the practical failure mode directly: "the test isn't defined when there are no consecutive exceedances at all, which is common," and explicitly, "as these are inherently rare events, the test has limited power."

**Transfer to Q4/Q11/Q14.** The independence/clustering question has a real analogue for this project (does the true on-field-review rate move with match stakes/stage — SF/final vs. group stage — the way the project's own pricing note already assumes, "SF scrutiny" nudging the estimate up in `matches/France Vs Spain (Jul.14.26)/04_rules_applied.json` Q4), but Christoffersen's test needs *even more* clustered-pair observations than Kupiec needs raw exceedances, because it is testing a second-order property (whether hits following hits deviate from the base rate) on top of the first-order one. If Kupiec already cannot be run meaningfully on this project's sample, Christoffersen categorically cannot.

### B.3 Modern treatments of the low-power problem in rare-event verification, and the meteorology "minimum sample" literature

**Bradley, Schwartz & Hashino (2008), "Sampling Uncertainty and Confidence Intervals for the Brier Score and Brier Skill Score," *Weather and Forecasting* 23(5):992-1006.** Already cited at survey level in `ML_MODEL_BUILD_RESEARCH_2026-07-13.md` B.8 (via the emergentmind overview); this session I attempted the primary AMS-hosted PDF directly and it was paywalled/blocked to automated fetch (returned an HTML redirect, not the PDF). What is reportable, from a secondary synthesis returned by web search this session (**not primary-text-verified — flagged explicitly**): "For rare-event occurrences with a true [skill score] of 0.4, the expected skill score is 0.34 or less for a sample of fewer than a hundred forecast-observation pairs... large sample sizes (n > 1000) are required for higher-skill forecasts of relatively rare events, whereas only quite modest sample sizes are needed for low-skill forecasts of common events." If accurate, this is directly on point: n≈100 (this project's entire WC2026 match count) sits exactly at the low end this secondary summary calls insufficient for the Brier Skill Score itself to stabilize on a *rare*-event target — independent of Q4/Q11/Q14 specifically, this would mean the project's own headline evaluation metric (Brier/RBP) is not yet reliable at this sample size for any low-base-rate question family, which is a stronger and more general claim than anything about Q4 alone. Given the paywall, I am reporting this as an unverified secondary claim, not asserting it as confirmed.

**Extreme Dependency Score (Stephenson, Casati et al. 2008, *Meteorological Applications* 15(1):41-50) and the Extremal Dependence Index / Symmetric EDI (Ferro & Stephenson 2011, *Weather and Forecasting* 26(5)).** Source, from search-result abstracts and a CAWCR (Australian Bureau of Meteorology forecast-verification research group) summary page read directly (https://www.cawcr.gov.au/projects/verification/Casati/statxtr.htm). The motivating problem these papers solve: traditional categorical verification scores (equitable threat score, Heidke skill score, etc.) mathematically *degenerate to zero or a trivial constant* as an event's base rate goes to zero, regardless of the forecaster's actual skill — meaning the score itself becomes uninformative for sufficiently rare events, independent of sample size. EDS and its refinements (EDI, SEDI) were built to converge to a real, non-degenerate skill value even as the base rate → 0. Source, directly quoted from the CAWCR page: "extreme events are often rare events: these are characterized by a small sample, and therefore large uncertainties," and the page's own recommended mitigation is "pooling in space and time" — explicitly flagged in the same source as introducing its own problem, "inhomogeneity and non-stationarity" (i.e., pooling data from conditions that are not really the same event-generating process). **Synthesis:** this is the meteorology literature independently arriving at the same tradeoff this project already knows from `PREREGISTRATION_cards_corners_offsides_and_combined.md`'s offsides saga and from the ML4T pooling discipline in `ML_MODEL_BUILD_RESEARCH_2026-07-13.md` — pooling more data buys statistical power but only if the pooled data-generating process is genuinely the same, and verifying that homogeneity assumption is itself hard. I searched directly for an explicit minimum-sample-size number in the EDS/EDI papers and did not find one stated as a number; the literature's answer is qualitative (pool, and use a score that doesn't degenerate) rather than a stated "N > X."

**Hitchens, Brooks & Kay (2013), "Objective Limits on Forecasting Skill of Rare Events," *Weather and Forecasting* 28(2):525-534, and its 2001 conference-paper predecessor (Brooks & Kay, obtained directly, https://www.nssl.noaa.gov/users/brooks/public_html/papers/prague2k1.pdf).** Source, read directly from the 2001 predecessor paper (the 2013 journal version was not reachable behind the AMS paywall this session; the methodology is the same "practically perfect" forecast concept the 2013 paper is built on, per the abstract). The core idea: even a forecaster with *perfect advance knowledge* of a rare event's true occurrence cannot score a perfect 1.0 on standard verification metrics, because operational constraints (minimum product size, minimum report thresholds) force some irreducible false-alarm/missed-detection rate even under perfect information. The paper's contribution is to compute this "practically perfect" ceiling/floor and use it as the real benchmark, rather than the theoretical 0/1 extremes. **Synthesis, transfer to this project:** this is a genuinely useful reframing for Q4/Q11/Q14 evaluation. Even a hypothetically perfect Q4 forecaster (one who somehow knew the true, unknowable-in-advance probability exactly) would not "beat" every realization of the crowd on a single-shot RBP basis, because RBP rewards the realized outcome, not the correctness of the underlying probability — the appropriate benchmark for "is this project's Q4 number any good" is not "did it score well on the one live match" but "is the number consistent with the best obtainable base-rate estimate," which is a calibration question, not a single-outcome question. A separate NHESS 2022 paper on rare/severe-event verification methodology (Wilks or a related author; retrieved via `nhess.copernicus.org`, read directly this session) makes a structurally identical point about the "accuracy paradox": for base rates this low, trivial always-predict-"no" forecasts score deceptively well on raw accuracy, and only chance-corrected, direction-of-fit-consistent scores (the paper argues specifically for the Peirce skill score over the more commonly used Heidke score) avoid rewarding a skill-less forecaster — but that same source paper, read directly, states plainly that it does **not** solve, and does not attempt to solve, "the underlying sampling problem that makes any verification score unreliable when events are truly rare." That sentence is as close to a direct answer to this project's Task C question as anything found in this literature search, and it comes from a paper whose entire purpose is scoring-rule selection for rare events, not sample-size adequacy — meaning even papers trying to solve the rare-event verification problem concede the sampling problem is separate and unsolved by better scoring rules alone.

**Is there a MinTRL-equivalent for calibration of rare binary events?** Explicitly searched for this and did not find a named, citable equivalent to Bailey & López de Prado's Minimum Track Record Length (already covered for Sharpe ratios in `ML_MODEL_BUILD_RESEARCH_2026-07-13.md`'s ML4T citations). What exists instead, gathered across B.1-B.3 above, is a *scattered but convergent* set of numeric anchors that functions as a de facto answer: Kupiec's own worked example needs ~255 i.i.d. trials to get 65% power against a 3x rate misspecification (B.1); the Brier Skill Score secondary source above suggests n>1000 for reliable skill measurement of low-skill rare-event forecasts and calls n<100 unreliable even for a real 0.4-skill forecaster (B.3, unverified primary); Christoffersen-style clustering tests need strictly more than that because they are testing a second-order property (B.2). None of these numbers were derived for football, and none constitute a single formally-named "minimum track record length for calibration," but they triangulate to the same qualitative place: **the reliable-detection sample size for a rare binary event's calibration is in the many hundreds to low thousands of trials, not the tens to low hundreds this project will ever accumulate for a WC2026-specific rare event.**

### B.4 Time-to-event / race / competing-risks forecasts (Q14's structure)

**Harrell's concordance index (C-index) and survival-model calibration.** Source, synthesized from multiple search-result summaries this session (a Sebastian Pölsterl blog post on evaluating survival models, and a 2023 *Statistics in Medicine* paper, Hartman et al., "Pitfalls of the concordance index for survival outcomes" — full text of the latter not fetched, working from the search-result abstract/summary only, flagged as such). The C-index measures discrimination: for a randomly chosen *comparable pair* of subjects, does the model assign higher predicted risk to whichever one actually experienced the event first. It is fundamentally a **pairwise** statistic — it needs many comparable pairs to estimate with any precision, and its own small-sample behavior is documented as poor: one summary reports that "in a small synthetic dataset of 100 samples, estimates of the concordance index were on average below the actual value... and the variance of differences was quite big," with additional bias introduced by censoring (subjects whose event time is never observed within the study window).

**Synthesis — why this machinery does not transfer to a single Q14-style question, even in principle.** Q14 ("will Spain make the first substitution") is structurally a two-way race (competing risks: team A subs first vs. team B subs first, analogous to a two-cause survival/duration model), which is exactly the class of problem survival analysis and C-index methodology were built for. But the entire apparatus — C-index, calibration plots for survival curves, IPCW-adjusted concordance under censoring — requires a *population* of race realizations (many matches, each contributing one race outcome and, ideally, both teams' actual substitution times) to estimate discrimination or calibration with a usable confidence interval. This project's `ml/backtests/rare_event_panel.csv` (built already in the repo, read directly this session — see the grounding note in Task C below) *does* have that population: 100 WC2026 matches, each with a determined first-sub team and both teams' first-substitution minute where recorded. That is a real, moderately-sized dataset for a **retrospective base-rate / logistic-regression-style read** of "which factors predict subbing first" (e.g., trailing at a given minute, tactical profile) — but it is not enough to validate a genuinely *predictive* survival model's calibration in the Kupiec/Christoffersen or C-index sense, because the validation target (Spain's true underlying hazard-of-first-substitution function, conditional on covariates known only for this one match) has effectively n=1 for the actual live decision, however large the historical training panel is. The distinction matters: **the training data for a base-rate estimate can be n≈100 and be genuinely useful; validating that any particular fitted hazard model correctly forecasts a novel single match's race outcome cannot be done with any statistical confidence from one realization, no matter how good the historical panel is.** This is the same point B.1-B.3 make for Q4, arrived at from a different literature.

### B.5 Timing-window forecasts (Q11's structure) and point-process / intensity-model validation

**The genuine quant-finance parallel: self-exciting point processes for order-arrival modeling.** Source, synthesized from multiple search-result summaries on Hawkes-process methodology in market microstructure (Bowsher's original order-arrival Hawkes application; several arXiv papers on limit-order-book Hawkes models). The standard goodness-of-fit technique for any point-process intensity model (Hawkes or otherwise) is **Ogata's (1988) residual analysis via the random time change theorem**: transform the observed event times into "residuals" using the model's own fitted cumulative intensity function; if the fitted intensity is correct, these transformed residuals should behave like a *unit-rate Poisson process*, which is then checked (commonly via a QQ-plot of inter-residual gaps against the Exponential(1) distribution, or a formal test of that hypothesis).

**Synthesis — why this is the right conceptual frame for Q11 but cannot be run at this project's scale.** Q11 ("did a goal fall in the window between the first and second hydration break") is not really a rare-event question at all once tested against the project's own data — `ml/backtests/rare_event_panel.csv` (read directly, not modified, in this session) shows 74 of 99 WC2026 matches with both hydration breaks detected had a goal in that window (≈74.7% empirical rate), essentially confirming the FRA-ESP pricing note's own simulation-derived estimate of 0.71 (`matches/France Vs Spain (Jul.14.26)/05_estimates.json`, Q11). So Q11 is best modeled as *reading a fixed probability off an already-fitted intensity process* (goals arrive via a time-varying-rate point process across the match; a ~40-45-minute window capturing over 40% of an average 90-minute match's playing time should indeed carry roughly window-share-plus-a-bit of the total goal probability, consistent with the observed rate) rather than as a rare, hard-to-validate event. Ogata-style residual analysis would be the textbook-correct way to validate the *underlying within-match goal-intensity model* (not the single "did it happen" indicator) if the project ever fit one — that is a legitimate, data-supportable exercise, because there are hundreds of individual goal events across the 100-match WC2026 panel (and the additional StatsBomb 2018/2022 shot-timestamp data) to build a residual QQ-plot from. What Ogata's method does **not** do, and cannot do, is validate the *binary* "did a goal fall in this specific one-off 40-minute window in this specific match" question with any per-match confidence — that is a single Bernoulli draw from the (validatable) underlying intensity process, and no amount of validating the intensity process converts a single future draw into a low-variance forecast. This is a genuinely different, better-off situation than Q4: the *model* (goal-timing intensity) is validatable with the data this project has; the specific match's *realization* of that model is not, and was never going to be, regardless of validation quality — that is just irreducible single-match variance, not a data-scarcity problem.

## Task C — Recommended methodology, and an honest power assessment, per question type

### C.0 Grounding: what this project's own historical data actually contains for these three questions

Before designing a backtest it matters what is actually countable. `ml/backtests/build_rare_event_panels.py` (read directly this session, not modified or executed) already built `ml/backtests/rare_event_panel.csv` from all 100 played WC2026 matches' ESPN commentary/event feeds, and its output (also read directly, not modified) gives real counts rather than the "maybe 1-in-10-to-15" estimate this task was framed around:

- **Q4 (on-field VAR review).** ESPN's commentary feed mentions "VAR" in some form in 51 of 100 WC2026 matches — but the script's own docstring is explicit that this is a *necessary overcount*: ESPN's feed records "VAR Decision: X" whenever the booth reviews a call, whether or not the referee actually walked to the pitchside monitor (the specific act Q4 asks about), and the two cannot be distinguished from this data source. The project's own FRA/ESP match-prep note (`matches/France Vs Spain (Jul.14.26)/03_model_derivations.json`, `key_form_facts.var_events`) records a hand-checked count for the two finalists' own twelve combined 2026 matches: "VAR decisions in 7/12 games but only **1** near-certain ON-FIELD review (FRA-PAR penalty award 67')." One true realization in twelve matches, hand-verified, is the actual evidentiary basis this project has for Q4 — not a machine-countable rate across the full 100-match field, because the raw data cannot separate booth-only checks from monitor reviews at scale.
- **Q11 (goal between hydration breaks).** 74 of 99 matches with both breaks detected had a goal fall in that window (≈74.7%), closely matching the project's own simulation-derived FRA-ESP estimate of 0.71. This is, contrary to how the task briefing frames it, **not actually a low-base-rate problem** — it is a well-measured, high-base-rate timing-window question with a full 99-match empirical panel behind it. Its real difficulty (documented in the script's own header comment, describing a caught bug where a naive version of the extraction conflated injury stoppages with genuine hydration breaks and understated the true rate by roughly half) is *window-definition fragility*, not sample scarcity.
- **Q14 (first substitution race).** All 100 matches have a determined first-substituting team and, in most rows, both teams' first-substitution minute. This is also not sample-starved in the raw-count sense — the issue (per B.4 above) is that a large *historical* panel of race outcomes supports a base-rate/logistic read of "which factors correlate with subbing first," but does not, and cannot, validate a specific predictive claim about this one live match's race with any real confidence, because the live target has an effective n of one.

**This changes the honest verdict per question, and it is worth stating plainly: only Q4 is a genuine "sample size is the whole problem" case in the classical rare-event-backtesting sense described in Task B. Q11 and Q14 have adequate historical panels for a base-rate read; their problems are generalizability (window definition, match-to-match heterogeneity) and structural (a race target that is inherently single-shot at decision time), not raw scarcity.**

### C.1 Q4-style rare binary events: recommended methodology

Given B.1's Kupiec numbers (255 i.i.d. trials needed for only 65% power against a *3x* rate misspecification) and the project's actual usable evidence for genuine on-field reviews (1 hand-verified occurrence in 12 matches, and no reliable machine-countable denominator beyond that because the underlying data source cannot distinguish booth checks from monitor reviews), no frequentist hypothesis test of this project's own design or calibration is possible for Q4 this tournament. Kupiec's test, run naively on "1 success in 12 trials" (or even optimistically on "51 apparent VAR mentions in 100 matches," which is the wrong quantity entirely), would have essentially no power to distinguish a correctly-calibrated 8% base rate from a badly miscalibrated 20% or 3% one — the confidence interval on a Clopper-Pearson exact binomial interval for 1/12 is wide by construction (roughly 0.2%-40% at 95% coverage for this small an n; the interval is not narrow enough to discipline a specific point estimate like 0.35).

What is legitimate: an explicit, small-sample-safe **Bayesian point estimate with an informative prior**, not a validated model. Concretely — and this is exactly what the project's own FRA-ESP pricing note already did, whether or not it was framed this way — combine (a) a prior from published WC-era on-field-review base rates (if any exist externally; not independently re-verified in this session, flagged), (b) the hand-checked 1/12 rate from this project's own most relevant subset (the two finalists' own matches), and (c) a stage-adjustment (semifinal stakes plausibly raising scrutiny/review likelihood, and extra-time exposure adding a second window of opportunity) — and report the result as a domain-reasoned point estimate with explicit low confidence, never as a "backtested" number. The project's own `04_rules_applied.json` note for Q4 ("no settled precedent anywhere in the record... nudged to 0.35 acknowledging the crowd likely sits higher and this is our lowest-confidence number") is, read against this literature, already doing the statistically honest thing: producing a reasoned prior instead of either skipping the question or dressing up a 1-in-12 count as a validated rate. The one concrete addition this document recommends: if the project wants a real confidence band around that number for internal record-keeping (not for the submission itself), a **Beta-Binomial posterior with a weakly informative prior** (e.g. Beta(1,10), reflecting a prior belief the true rate is low, single-digit-percent, updated with the 1-in-12 hand-checked observation) gives a defensible small-sample interval; a raw Clopper-Pearson interval on n=12 does not, because it assumes no prior information and produces an interval too wide to be actionable.

### C.2 Q11-style timing-window forecasts: recommended methodology

Because this question type is genuinely data-rich (C.0 above), the honest methodology is different in kind from Q4's, and closer to legitimate walk-forward validation. Two levels are separable, exactly as B.5 (Ogata residual-analysis discussion) lays out:

First, the **within-match goal-intensity process** (when in a match do goals tend to occur, and does that rate vary by match state, stage, or team identity) is validatable with this project's actual data — hundreds of individual goal-minute observations across the 100-match WC2026 panel plus the StatsBomb 2018/2022 shot-and-goal-timestamp history. A walk-forward design in the same spirit as the already-completed and passed SOT/corners hierarchical backtests (`PREREGISTRATION_sot_hierarchical_backtest.md`, `PREREGISTRATION_cards_corners_offsides_and_combined.md`) is directly applicable here: fit a time-varying intensity (or a simpler piecewise-constant window-share model) on strictly-prior matches, predict the next match's goal-timing distribution, score with a proper scoring rule (log-likelihood of observed goal minutes under the fitted intensity, or Ogata-style residual analysis pooling across many matches' worth of goals), and gate deployment on the same match-clustered-bootstrap promotion criterion the project already applies.

Second, the **binary realization for one specific match's specific window** ("did a goal fall in FRA-ESP's own hydration-break window") is a single draw from that validated process and cannot itself be separately backtested with any per-match confidence, no matter how good the underlying intensity model is — this is the same point B.5 makes and it is a statement about irreducible variance, not about insufficient data. The correct posture: validate the *model* (which this project's data supports), then accept that any one match's outcome will look like noise around that model's prediction, exactly as it would for a genuinely well-calibrated goals-scored Poisson prediction on a single match. The project should not expect a "validated Q11 number" to reliably beat a single realization any more than a well-calibrated Poisson total-goals model reliably "wins" every individual match's total-goals question — calibration is a population property, not a per-match guarantee, and conflating the two is a distinct mistake from the pure-sample-scarcity problem Q4 has.

### C.3 Q14-style race/ordering forecasts: recommended methodology

Given B.4's analysis, the historical panel (100 matches, both teams' first-sub minutes in most rows) supports exactly one legitimate use: a **retrospective, walk-forward-validated logistic or Cox-style read of which observable pre-match/in-match covariates correlate with subbing first** (trailing at a given minute is the obvious first candidate; team identity/tactical-culture effects are a second, weaker one given roster/manager churn across even a single team's own matches). This is structurally the same design-class as the hierarchical GLMM work already validated for SOT/corners — a domain-class model (n = matches or team-matches, predicting a real observed outcome) rather than a meta/calibration-class model, and per this project's own decision rule (`ML_MODEL_BUILD_RESEARCH_2026-07-13.md` C.5), that is the class of problem worth attempting at this n.

What it cannot support, per B.4, is validating that any such fitted model's predicted probability for *this one live match* is well-calibrated in the Kupiec/C-index sense — a single race realization contributes exactly one bit of information to any subsequent validation exercise, so "was the model right this time" is unanswerable as a statistical claim about the model's skill, only as an anecdote. The honest deliverable for Q14 is therefore: a base-rate-plus-covariate estimate (e.g., team-level first-sub tendency shrunk toward the field-wide ~50/50 split, adjusted for observable match-state factors if a walk-forward-validated model clears the bar), reported with the same humility the project already uses elsewhere for genuinely evidence-thin questions, not a claim of validated predictive skill on the live match.

### C.4 Cross-cutting: is there a legitimate small-sample-safe approach at all?

Yes, but it is a change in what is being claimed, not a way to make the existing claim more defensible. Three concrete, legitimate small-sample tools, all already gestured at in earlier sections and consistent with this project's own existing practice (`ML_EXPERIMENTS_NOTEBOOK.md`'s bootstrap-CI discipline, cited in `ML_MODEL_BUILD_RESEARCH_2026-07-13.md` B.8):

1. **Exact/Bayesian interval estimation instead of hypothesis testing.** For Q4-style single-digit-count events, report a Clopper-Pearson or (preferably, given how wide Clopper-Pearson is at n=12) a Beta-Binomial posterior interval around the point estimate, explicitly framed as "the range consistent with the data," not a pass/fail test of any specific hypothesized rate. This does not create statistical power that does not exist; it correctly represents the uncertainty instead of hiding it behind a point estimate.
2. **Pooling across genuinely comparable question instances, with the homogeneity assumption stated and checked, not assumed.** The EDS/EDI literature's own recommended mitigation (B.3) — pool across space/time — transfers directly: pooling on-field-review counts across all knockout-stage 2026 matches (not just FRA/ESP's own 12) would widen the n modestly; pooling further across 2018/2022 StatsBomb-annotated matches (if VAR-review flags exist in that data — not verified this session, a concrete next step if pursued) would widen it further. Each pooling step should be accompanied by exactly the kind of check this project already knows how to do (the offsides-bug postmortem in `PREREGISTRATION_cards_corners_offsides_and_combined.md` is a direct precedent for "verify the pooled data sources actually measure the same thing before trusting the pooled rate").
3. **Domain-class over meta-class, and shrinkage over discrimination, exactly as C.5 of the existing ML4T-based research document already concludes for the SOT/cards/corners families.** Nothing in this document overturns that conclusion; it reinforces it for a harder case. A shrunk base rate, explicitly labeled as a base rate and not a validated model, is the correct tool precisely because the alternative (a fitted, "backtested" model) would be spending statistical power the data does not have, exactly as the project's own four prior meta-model rejections already demonstrated for a less extreme small-n regime (`ML_MODEL_BUILD_RESEARCH_2026-07-13.md` C.2).

## Direct answer to the practical question

Should this project trust a "backtest-validated" number for Q4/Q11/Q14-style questions this tournament, or not?

**No, not in the sense of a statistically validated model — and this differs by question in an important way that the framing of this task did not initially assume.** For Q4 specifically, the honest conclusion is exactly what Kupiec's own worked numbers say: with a hand-verified count of one true on-field review in twelve matches and no reliable way to extend that count across the wider field, no test in this literature — not Kupiec's POF, not Christoffersen's conditional-coverage extension, not a naive Brier/BSS comparison — has any meaningful power to confirm or reject this project's 0.35 estimate, or any other specific number, as "validated." Kupiec's own paper shows that even 255 i.i.d. daily observations (a full regulatory year, and roughly 2.5 times this project's entire tournament match count) yields only 65% power against a *severe* 3x rate misspecification; this project has roughly one-twentieth of that sample for the specific quantity Q4 asks about. The correct posture for Q4 is exactly what the project's own pricing note already did without naming it this way: a domain-reasoned Bayesian point estimate built from the best available small-sample evidence and external priors, submitted with explicitly low confidence, and never described internally or externally as "backtested." For Q11 and Q14, the situation is better but the same caution applies at the per-match level: both have adequate historical panels (99 and 100 matches respectively) to support a genuinely walk-forward-validated *domain model* of the underlying process (goal-timing intensity; substitution-timing covariates), in the same successfully-precedented style as the project's already-passed SOT and corners hierarchical backtests — but even a well-validated model of that kind cannot be verified against any single live match's outcome, because that is one realization of a population-level calibration claim, not an independent test of it. The single most important practical implication: price Q4 from explicit, humble domain reasoning and stop there; for Q11 and Q14, it is legitimate to build and walk-forward-validate an underlying process model using the existing panels if the time exists to do so properly (pre-registered, per the project's own C.5 rule), but do not expect — and do not claim, in any postmortem — that either the model or its absence will be "confirmed" or "refuted" by how this one match's Q11/Q14 questions happen to resolve.
