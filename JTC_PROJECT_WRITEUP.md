# Forecasting the 2026 World Cup: Models, Crowd-Bias Rules, and a Live Track Record on the Jump Probability Cup

*A solo research writeup of an ongoing forecasting-tournament campaign on play.sportspredict.com (the "Jump Probability Cup" / JTC).*

---

## 1. Abstract / TL;DR

This is a writeup of an active, solo research project built around the Jump Probability Cup (JTC), a World Cup 2026 match-prediction tournament on play.sportspredict.com, scored by a proprietary weighted-Brier-style metric the project calls **RBP** (Relative Brier Points — a score that is positive when the submitted forecast beats the platform's own crowd-consensus forecast on the same question, and negative when it loses to the crowd).

The project combines: (1) a from-scratch statistical modeling stack — point-in-time Elo ratings, a Poisson-regression goal model, a Dixon-Coles low-score correction, a Negative-Binomial overdispersion correction, and a separate ordered-logit match-result model — built and backtested against 49,000+ international matches since 1872; (2) a literature review of eight academic papers spanning goal-based (Poisson/bivariate-Poisson), rating-based (Elo/ordered-logit), scoring-rule-verification, and wisdom-of-crowds modeling traditions; (3) a tiered data-source reliability audit built empirically from match outcomes, not assumptions; (4) an empirically-derived "crowd-bias" correction model, expressed as `crowd ≈ 0.514 + 0.61×(our_estimate − 0.5)`, with more than a dozen named, evidence-graded situational rules layered on top; and (5) a small machine-learning/calibration layer (a flat feature matrix plus a Platt-scaling diagnostic).

As of the most recent rebuild of the master question dataset (`datasets/master_question_dataset.csv`, built directly from the raw per-match JSON files), the campaign has submitted real platform predictions across **49 settled matches and 436 individually-scored questions**, for a cumulative total of **+872.13 RBP**. [NEEDS VERIFICATION: this total is computed directly from the dataset CSV by this writeup's author at write time — it has not been independently cross-checked against a live platform export, and it differs from an earlier separate narrative tally of "+812.15 across 423 questions" reported in `STEPS_FOR_HIGH_POINTS.md` (dated 2026-06-26) and from an even earlier "+297.70 across ~125 questions" figure in older memory notes — see the discrepancy note at the end of Section 8.] The single best result on record is Germany 7–1 Curaçao (+72.47 RBP, 9/10 questions beating the crowd); the single worst is Brazil 1–1 Morocco, where a researched 0.58–0.60 estimate was overridden at submission time to a gut call of 1.00 and lost −51.97 RBP on a drawn match.

The project's most distinctive finding is not a single statistical model but an operational one: across hundreds of graded questions, the platform's crowd consensus is *structurally compressed toward 50%* relative to whatever the true probability looks like — a textbook favorite-longshot misperception effect, now fit as an explicit, regularly-revalidated regression. Exploiting that compression, not out-modeling the crowd on raw accuracy, is where most of the campaign's edge has come from.

## 2. Motivation & Background

### 2.1 What play.sportspredict.com is

SportsPredict is a web platform (marketing site at `sportspredict.com`, the actual logged-in app at `play.sportspredict.com`) that runs prediction tournaments scored against other participants. The Jump Probability Cup (JTC) is one such tournament, built around the 2026 FIFA World Cup. Concretely, the platform's structure (reverse-engineered via browser network interception in `bot/API_EXPLORATION_2026-06-27.md`) is:

- An **event** (JTC has its own event UUID) contains one or more **lobbies** — for example a "Group Stage" lobby — each with its own leaderboard.
- Each World Cup **match** has an associated set of **markets** (typically ~10 per match): a match-winner market, and a batch of secondary "prop" markets (total goals over/under, both-teams-to-score, corners, cards, offsides, individual player shots-on-target, etc.).
- Participants submit a probability (0–100%) for each market before it closes. After the match, the platform reveals the **crowd consensus** (the aggregate of all participants' submissions) for that market alongside the actual outcome, and scores every participant against both the outcome and, implicitly, against the field via the RBP mechanic.
- The platform exposes two separate API surfaces: a documented external/bot namespace (`api.sportspredict.com/api/v1`, two working routes — `/matches` and `/markets`) and an internal, browser-only, cookie-authenticated namespace used by the web app itself (`api.sportspredict.com/api`, unversioned) that additionally exposes settled-trade history, leaderboard rank, and per-user score summaries. The project's own data-collection bot currently uses the documented `v1` namespace for fetching open matches/markets; the internal namespace was explored as a way to automate the currently-manual collection of settled results and rank, per `bot/API_EXPLORATION_2026-06-27.md` (status: research complete, automated dashboard fetch not yet built as of that document).

### 2.2 What RBP measures, and why it matters

JTC's scoring metric, "Relative Brier Points" (RBP), behaves as a **relative, weighted Brier score**: for each question, both the participant's submitted probability and the platform's crowd-consensus probability are scored against the realized outcome using a Brier-style squared-error rule, and RBP is (a function of) the *difference* — positive when the participant beats the crowd, negative when the crowd wins. [NEEDS VERIFICATION: the project's own ledger analysis explicitly notes the precise functional form and per-question scale constant are not published by the platform and have been only partially reverse-engineered — see the honesty check in Section 8 and `project_crowd_bias_model.md`'s note that "SportsPredict likely weights by a per-question stake/liquidity factor we don't have."]

The Brier score is a **proper scoring rule**: the participant's expected score is uniquely maximized by reporting their true belief, not by hedging or sandbagging. This matters for the whole project's design — it means the right strategy is never "guess what wins," it's "report the best calibrated probability you can construct," and any deviation from that (see the gut-override loss discussed in Section 8) is a strategy error, not a legitimate alternate approach.

A second wrinkle, addressed directly in the literature review (Section 3) and revisited in Section 7, is that football outcomes are not simple binary events — a three-way market (Home win / Draw / Away win) has an *ordinal* structure (a draw sits "between" a home win and an away win on the goal-difference axis), and naive Brier scoring of three-way outcomes does not respect that ordering the way the Ranked Probability Score (RPS) does. Whether JTC's "weighted Brier" formula behaves more like plain Brier or more like RPS in practice has not been independently confirmed against the live platform and is treated as an open question (Section 11).

### 2.3 Why this is a genuinely hard, interesting forecasting problem

Three things make World Cup match forecasting unusually difficult relative to club football, the domain most of the academic literature in Section 3 was built for:

1. **Tiny in-tournament samples.** Teams play at most 3 group matches before elimination is possible; multi-season club-league panels (the substrate for Dixon-Coles, bivariate Poisson, etc.) don't exist at the tournament level.
2. **Long gaps between observations.** International teams play infrequently outside major tournaments, and squad composition changes meaningfully between World Cups — a four-year-old Elo rating is a much weaker signal than a four-year-old club rating would be.
3. **Format-specific crowd dynamics.** Unlike a real-money exchange (Polymarket, Smarkets, Pinnacle), JTC's crowd consensus is built from free, Brier-scored, no-liquidity-discipline probability guesses from a self-selected population of participants — closer to the casual-bettor populations studied in the national-sentiment/fan-bias literature (Section 3, Section 6) than to a sharp financial market. This single fact turns out to be the project's central exploitable edge.

This project situates itself relative to two existing forecasting genres: prediction-market/forecasting-community platforms like Good Judgment Open and Metaculus (which track calibration and running score over many resolved questions, the format this writeup borrows for Sections 7–8), and sports-analytics methodology writeups like FiveThirtyEight's Soccer Power Index and clubelo.com's Elo system (which popularized translating Poisson/Elo math into plain language for a general audience, the register this writeup tries to maintain throughout).

## 3. Literature Review

The project's modeling choices are grounded in a deliberate literature review of eight sources (`research_notes.md`), organized below by modeling lineage rather than by publication date, following the convention used in the CS229 student reports surveyed during this writeup's structure research.

### 3.1 Goal-based / Poisson-family models

**Dixon & Coles (1997), "Modelling Association Football Scores and Inefficiencies in the Football Betting Market"** (`papers/` — not directly held; reconstructed via the Sarmanov-family paper and secondary sources, see 3.1 caveat below) is the foundational model in this lineage. It treats home and away goals as two **independent Poisson distributions**, each with a rate built from a team's attack strength, the opponent's defensive weakness, and a home-advantage multiplier (in log-linear form, this is literally a Poisson regression with team dummy variables). Its two enduring contributions are (a) the **"tau" correction**, a small multiplicative adjustment applied only to the four low-scoring cells (0-0, 1-0, 0-1, 1-1) to fix independent-Poisson's tendency to under-predict draws and low scorelines, governed by a single dependence parameter ρ (typically small and negative, around −0.05 to −0.20 in club football); and (b) **exponential time-decay weighting** of historical matches, so a team's current rating reflects recent form rather than the full multi-season history equally.

**Karlis & Ntzoufras (2003), "Analysis of Sports Data by Using Bivariate Poisson Models"** (`papers/karlis_ntzoufras_2003.pdf`, fully read) goes a step further: rather than nudging four cells after the fact, it models the home/away goal correlation directly via a genuine **bivariate Poisson distribution** built from three latent independent Poisson components, fit via an EM algorithm. A further refinement — the **diagonal-inflated bivariate Poisson** — adds a finite mixture term specifically to fix residual draw under-prediction. The paper's one structural limitation, relevant to international football: the base bivariate-Poisson model can only capture *positive* home/away goal correlation, whereas real data (especially internationally and in women's football) sometimes shows meaningfully *negative* correlation.

**Michels, Ötting & Karlis (2023), "Extending the Dixon and Coles Model"** (`papers/extending_dixon_coles_2307.02139.pdf`) reframes the Dixon-Coles tau correction as a special case of the broader **Sarmanov family** of bivariate distributions, and shows empirically (motivated by women's football, where negative goal correlation runs stronger, around −0.25 to −0.40) that DC's tau correction can only reach a narrow range of achievable correlation (roughly −0.05 to −0.08 for typical international-football scoring rates) — i.e., "good but limited" for any competition with stronger-than-usual goal dependence.

**Crowder, Dixon, Ledford & Robinson (2002), "Dynamic Modelling and Prediction of English Football League Matches for Betting"** (not held in `papers/`; reconstructed from secondary/abstract sources only) extends the DC97 framework by letting attack/defence parameters evolve over time as a state-space process rather than staying fixed for a season — the explicit "dynamic" analogue of Elo, but built on the Poisson-scoreline structure. A full from-scratch implementation was judged out of scope for this project; the *practical* substitute adopted instead is DC's own exponential time-decay (already part of the model, Section 5) combined with periodic re-fitting, which approximates "time-varying strength" without a full particle-filter or state-space estimation step.

### 3.2 Rating-based models feeding an outcome regression

**Hvattum & Arntzen (2010), "Using ELO Ratings for Match Result Prediction in Association Football"** (not held in `papers/`; reconstructed from secondary sources) is the direct template for half of this project's modeling stack. It defines a standard chess-style Elo update (`K=20`, expected score from the logistic formula with divisor 400) plus a goals-based variant where the K-factor scales with margin of victory, then feeds the resulting **rating difference** into an **ordered logistic regression** (`MASS::polr()`-style, "proportional odds" model) that outputs Home/Draw/Away probabilities directly — calibrated against the actual three-way outcome rather than derived after the fact from a scoreline distribution. On 14 seasons / 30,524 English league matches, this approach beats uniform and historical-frequency baselines and even beats richer 50–100-covariate "kitchen sink" models (Goddard 2005), but **loses to bookmaker-implied probabilities** — a result echoed by nearly every other paper in this review, and the reason this project treats market prices as a primary, not secondary, input wherever they exist (Section 4, Section 9).

### 3.3 Scoring-rule verification and the calibration/resolution distinction

**Constantinou & Fenton (2012), "Solving the Problem of Inadequate Scoring Rules for Assessing Probabilistic Football Forecast Models"** (`papers/constantinou_fenton_2012.pdf`, fully read) is the source of the **Ranked Probability Score (RPS)**, built specifically to respect football's ordinal Home/Draw/Away structure — unlike plain Brier, which can in some configurations score a "confidently wrong about direction" forecaster no worse (or even better) than a merely "one category off" forecaster. The paper's five canonical benchmark scenarios are a useful diagnostic checklist that this project has not yet run against JTC's actual scoring formula (flagged as future work, Section 11) — until that is done, it remains an open question whether JTC's "weighted Brier" behaves more like plain Brier (in which case hedging toward Draw on genuine direction uncertainty is *not* specially rewarded) or more like RPS (in which case it is).

**Murphy (1973), "A New Vector Partition of the Probability Score"** (not held in `papers/`; reconstructed via the Foulley paper below, which states and uses it directly) supplies the foundational **Calibration-Refinement decomposition** of the Brier score: `E[Brier] = Uncertainty − Resolution + Reliability`, where Reliability is calibration error (lower is better) and Resolution is discriminative power (higher is better). This decomposition is the conceptual basis for this project's Section 7 calibration work and for distinguishing "I need a better model" (low resolution) from "I need to trust my model less" (high reliability/calibration error) — two different fixes that a single aggregate Brier number cannot distinguish.

**Foulley (2021), "More on Verification of Probability Forecasts for Football Outcomes"** (`papers/verification_prob_forecasts_2106.14345.pdf`, fully read, 23pp) is the single most directly actionable paper in the review. Applied to four seasons of UEFA Champions League group-stage data, it: (a) extends Murphy's decomposition with two further equivalent decompositions (Likelihood-Base and Yates 1982's five-component form); (b) gives a clean, directly-codeable estimation recipe for Reliability/Resolution/Uncertainty from real (forecast, outcome) pairs using isotonic-regression-based recalibration; (c) reports that Draw is structurally the hardest outcome to discriminate (Harrell's C-statistic ≈0.79–0.82 for Home/Away forecasts vs only ≈0.62 for Draw); and (d) derives a closed-form result that a Poisson model where both teams' scoring rates depend on the *same* rating-difference covariate (with opposite signs) **automatically induces negative home/away goal correlation** — no Dixon-Coles tau or bivariate-Poisson λ₃ term required. This last finding directly motivated this project's choice (Section 5) to feed Elo difference into the Poisson means directly, rather than relying solely on the tau correction.

### 3.4 Wisdom-of-crowds and aggregation

**"Wisdom of the Crowds: Forecasting the 2018 FIFA World Cup"** (`papers/wisdom_crowds_2018wc.pdf`, fully read, 32pp) is the closest prior real-world analogue to JTC itself: a public 2018 World Cup forecasting contest (fifaexperts.com), scored via Brier score, where many participants submitted Home/Draw/Away probabilities for all 64 matches. Its key findings, directly load-bearing for Section 6 of this writeup: simple unweighted averaging of many forecasters' probabilities ("Local Wisdom") and market-consensus aggregation ("Global Wisdom") both ranked **at or near the top** of the entire leaderboard, beating most individual participants; more sophisticated weighting schemes (top-N averaging, Budescu-Chen regret-minimization weighting, online "expert advice" algorithms) did **not** reliably beat simple averaging — added complexity didn't pay off. The paper also proves, via a geometric/polarization argument, that under Brier scoring the **maximin (worst-case-minimizing) forecast with zero information is the uniform forecast (1/3, 1/3, 1/3)** — a formal justification for this project's standing rule (Section 8, "Loss Pattern #2") against ever submitting 0% or 100% regardless of conviction.

### 3.5 Synthesis for this project

Three threads from this review run through every later section: (1) goal-based models (DC97/Karlis-Ntzoufras lineage) and rating-based models (Hvattum & Arntzen) are complementary, not competing — this project runs both and compares them (Section 5); (2) the Murphy/Foulley calibration-vs-resolution distinction is the right diagnostic frame for self-evaluating a forecasting record (Section 7); and (3) the wisdom-of-crowds literature's verdict that "the crowd/market is a very strong baseline, hard to beat outright" is precisely *why* this project's actual edge (Section 6) comes from modeling the crowd's specific, situational *deviations* from that baseline, not from out-forecasting it on raw accuracy.

## 4. Data — Sources, Pipeline, and Reliability

### 4a. Source inventory and reliability tiers

The single most distinctive data artifact in this project is a tiered source-reliability audit (`feedback_data_source_reliability.md`), built empirically from 26+ settled matches and 246+ graded questions (~729 cumulative RBP at the time it was last updated) — not asserted from first principles, but derived by tracing specific wins and losses back to which data source drove the decision. It is reproduced here close to verbatim because, per its own framing and this writeup's own check against public sports-data documentation exemplars (FiveThirtyEight's soccer-spi README, the Datasheets-for-Datasets standard, public GitHub football-data pipelines), no public exemplar surveyed during this writeup's preparation documents source reliability this granularly — most public pipelines stop at "data sourced from X API" with no tiering at all.

**Tier 1 — reliably profitable, use always, weight heavily:**

| Source | What it's used for | Why trusted | Known failure mode |
|---|---|---|---|
| GD1 (prior-group-match) individual player behavioral data, via ESPN's hidden site API / Opta / FotMob | Per-player and per-team offside, SOT, foul counts from a team's *own* prior match | The single most powerful source in the project; produced the largest individual wins (e.g., +22.07, +25.69, +20.74 on various offside/SOT props) | Fails hard when match context changes — same team's GD1 "calm" profile does not transfer once they are chasing a 3+ goal deficit (see Section 6, "blowout reversal") |
| Main sportsbook consensus (FanDuel + DraftKings + Betfair + bet365 agreeing) | Match winner, BTTS, major props | When 3+ major books agree within 5%, near-ground-truth; a player's *absence* from scorer markets across multiple main books is itself a strong signal (formalized as the "MAIN_BOOK_ABSENCE" rule: probability < 35% if absent from both FanDuel and DraftKings) | Following a niche aggregator (e.g., Goal.com) that disagrees with main-book absence has caused real losses (−11.03 RBP, Ramos/POR-UZB) |
| FootyMetrics referee career statistics (YC/game, pens/game) | Card and penalty questions | Large career sample (80+ matches) per referee | Needs a blowout discount (30–40%) when an extreme mismatch is in play |
| Club-level SOT/90 from the top-5 European leagues | Individual player SOT props | Reliable for top-5-league players; Saudi Pro League / MLS-tier data carries roughly 60% the weight | Even correct club-rate data can fail for a specific match (variance, not bias) |
| Player's World-Cup-specific scoring record vs. overall career rate | Individual scorer props | Tournament performance is measurably different from career performance; when the gap exceeds ~0.10, weight the WC-specific rate 70%, career rate 30% | — |
| Polymarket (>$1M traded) | Match-winner probability | Financially incentivized, high-liquidity participants | Below $500K volume, only a minor signal |

**Tier 2 — useful with specific adjustments, never primary driver alone:** footystats 10-match foul-rate averages (reliable for *magnitude*, not *direction*, without first predicting which team defends); market over/under and BTTS lines for total-goals calibration; trend/preview sites (Football Whispers, Racing Post — directional only, 15–20% weight cap); the project's own ordered-logit/Poisson models (reliable for match structure, but requiring heavy market-weighting — 90% market / 10% model — once the Elo gap between two teams exceeds 350 points, since the model systematically underestimates blowout favorites at that range).

**Tier 3 — unreliable, avoid or heavily discount:** niche aggregator scorer odds when a player is absent from main books (the Goal.com/Ramos failure above); GD1 behavioral data applied naively to extreme blowout scenarios (4+ goal deficits override tactical history every time a team abandons its shape to chase); base-rate foul data used without first predicting which team will be defending; personal conviction overriding a researched number (the project's two worst-ever single results, −51.97 and −44.55, both trace to this failure mode — see Section 8); and cross-team GD1 extrapolation (using Team A's prior-match behavior to predict Team B's behavior in an unrelated fixture).

The audit's stated master hierarchy, used whenever sources conflict: verified GD1 individual behavioral data (with a hard floor of 0.25 on any count-stat question, regardless of how low the data points) > main sportsbook consensus > referee career stats > club-level SOT data > WC-specific career record > high-volume Polymarket > GD1 team-level stats > footystats foul averages (magnitude only) > trend sites (sanity check only).

### 4b. Pipeline architecture

The data pipeline runs in three loosely-coupled stages, in the same spirit as the staging→intermediate→mart pattern used by public football-data pipelines (e.g., `dcaribou/transfermarkt-datasets`, `paolomagni/football-platform`) surveyed during this writeup's structural research, though built independently and not against that pattern explicitly:

- **Raw fetch (immutable).** `bot/fetch_markets.py` pulls all open matches and their markets from the SportsPredict `v1` API and writes them to disk immediately as JSONL — one line per match's market response, deliberately never accumulating the full result set in memory before saving (a standing operational rule: API responses are written to disk the instant they arrive, never batched in RAM, after this project twice lost data to a mid-run script failure before that rule was adopted). `bot/fetch_dashboard.py` is the (research-complete, not yet fully automated) counterpart intended to pull settled results, rank, and score summaries from the platform's internal web API. `bot/discover_endpoints.py` and the `bot/API_EXPLORATION_2026-06-27.md` session log document the reverse-engineering process that distinguished the two API namespaces (Section 2.1). `bot/scrape_competitors.py` separately pulls publicly-visible competitor trade/market data within the same lobby for competitive benchmarking. Every per-match research record (Elo/Poisson inputs, Smarkets market prices, applied rules, final submitted estimates, post-match outcomes) lives as one raw JSON file per match under `data/external_markets/`, and — per a standing project rule documented independently as `feedback_raw_data_immutable.md` — these raw files are **never edited or overwritten**; any reprocessing reads the originals and writes to a new, separate derived file.
- **Cleaning / classification.** `bot/classify_markets.py` sorts every fetched market question into the categories the existing models can or cannot price (`match_winner`, `total_goals_under/over`, `btts_and_over` are model-covered; everything else — cards, corners, offsides, player props — currently has "no_model" and is priced by the manual research/rules workflow in Section 6 instead). `bot/team_code_map.py` resolves SportsPredict's FIFA-style team codes to the team-name spellings used in the historical results panel, a small but necessary join-key reconciliation step. `bot/fetch_smarkets_mapping.py` builds a one-time mapping from Smarkets' World Cup 2026 event list to each match's full market list, used as the basis for pulling live market prices.
- **Derived datasets / mart.** `datasets/build_master_dataset.py` is the authoritative, read-only-against-raw build script: it reads every match file in `data/external_markets/*.json` (handling three distinct schema "eras" the raw files have accumulated as the project evolved) and writes one flat row per (match, question) to `datasets/master_question_dataset.csv` — the project's single closest analogue to a 538-style "data dictionary lives separately from the modeling code" artifact (see Section 4c). `ml/build_feature_matrix.py` builds a second, ML-oriented flat table (`ml/feature_matrix.csv`) handling five distinct schema variants encountered across the raw files' evolution, specifically for the Platt-scaling diagnostic in Section 7.

### 4c. Data dictionary

The full column-by-column documentation lives in `datasets/MASTER_DATASET_DICTIONARY.md` and is reproduced in summary in the Appendix (Section 12). In brief: `master_question_dataset.csv` has **480 rows** (one per match-question pair, validated as of this writeup's read of the file), **73 columns**, drawn from **49 source match files** under `data/external_markets/`, spanning match-level fields (team codes, dates, venue, altitude, pre-match Elo, rest days, squad value, FIFA ranking, which named RULEs fired) and question-level fields (our estimate, crowd estimate, outcome, RBP, beat-crowd flag, whether the question was ever actually submitted). Of those 480 rows, **436 have a recorded RBP value** (the remainder are questions that were researched but never reached the platform before a deadline, or belong to a calibration-only match with no real submission — see Section 8). A documented and load-bearing data-quality finding inside this dictionary: **question numbers (`Q1`, `Q2`, etc.) are not a reliable join key across different containers within the same raw file** — the dictionary's build script resolves this by topic-word-overlap matching rather than assuming positional consistency, after finding two concrete cases (`eng_cro_2026-06-17.json`, `cro_pan_2026-06-23.json`) where naive Q-number joining would have silently paired the wrong question's pre-match research to its post-match outcome.

A second, smaller derived table, `ml/feature_matrix.csv` (built separately by `ml/build_feature_matrix.py` for the ML/calibration work in Section 7), currently has **264 rows**, of which 246 carry a settled RBP, covering matches from 2026-06-11 through 2026-06-22 — an earlier, narrower slice of the same underlying raw files, reflecting the point in the project's timeline when the calibration diagnostic was last run (see Section 7's discrepancy note: this is the source of the 246-question figure that appears throughout the Platt-scaling analysis, distinct from the 436-question figure in the more complete, more recently rebuilt master dataset).

### 4d. Known caveats and deliberate exclusions

- **Squad value / age / FIFA-ranking enrichment columns are null for five teams** (Cape Verde, Curaçao, DR Congo, Haiti, Ivory Coast) — they are simply absent from the 119-row Transfermarkt extract used, not a join failure.
- **Pre-match "recommended estimate" enrichment only covers ~24% of rows** (114 of 480) — not every match has machine-readable pre-match research saved in a format the build script can confidently match to a specific post-match question; this is a genuine historical data gap (concentrated in matches before ~2026-06-18), not a matching-threshold artifact.
- **Per-match boxscore detail (shots/SOT/cards/corners/offsides) is not yet flattened into dataset columns** — it lives inside each raw file's evidence blocks in inconsistent per-era shapes, and flattening it was explicitly deferred as out of scope for the current dataset build (`DATASET_AUDIT_2026-06-26.md`).
- **The older cross-match "settled markets ledger" (`data/external_markets/settled_markets_ledger.json`) is explicitly stale** — last updated 2026-06-15, covering only the first 11 matches — and is documented in the project's own data audit as something that should **not** be used as an ML training source; `master_question_dataset.csv` is the current authoritative rebuild.
- **The historical rest-days feature file (`data/external/travel/rest_days_features.csv`) is stale**, stopping 2026-06-09, before the tournament started — the current dataset computes rest days directly from the match-results panel instead.

## 5. Methodology / Models

The project runs two parallel approaches to the core match-result question — a goals-based pipeline and a direct outcome-regression pipeline — plus supporting overdispersion and dependence corrections, following the "run both, compare" posture the literature review (Section 3.2) recommends.

### 5.1 Point-in-time Elo ratings (`model/elo.py`)

**Plain English:** every national team gets a single number representing its current strength, updated after every match using the standard chess-style Elo formula (a team gains rating points for beating a team it "should" have beaten by less than expected, or for any unexpected result, and loses points symmetrically). Three refinements specific to football are layered on: a **goal-difference multiplier** (a 4-0 win moves ratings more than a 1-0 win, but not linearly — a `(11+|diff|)/8` formula caps the effect of very large margins), a flat **home-advantage offset** (+100 rating points, applied only when the match is not on a neutral pitch), and a **match-importance weight** (K-factor of 60 for World Cup finals down to 20 for friendlies, via a lookup table). This is a deterministic, non-fitted formula — every team's rating at any date depends only on matches played strictly before that date, which is what makes it usable for genuine forward-looking forecasting (no lookahead). The system is computed from scratch against `martj42`'s 49,000+-match international results history (1872–present) rather than scraped from eloratings.net, specifically so the point-in-time discipline can be guaranteed.

**Technical note:** `INITIAL_RATING=1500` is a free parameter for every team's very first appearance in the dataset (1872 for the oldest teams) — by 2026 this barely matters for teams with a century of history, but matters more for recently-FIFA-affiliated nations, a limitation discussed further in Section 10.

### 5.2 Poisson goal-rate regression (`topics/match-winner-goals-totals/model/poisson_goals.py`)

**Plain English:** rather than treating Elo as a black box, this stage turns the Elo *difference* between two teams into an actual prediction of how many goals each side is expected to score, using a classical statistical regression (a Poisson GLM — the same family used by Dixon-Coles and Hvattum & Arntzen) rather than a black-box ML model: `log(expected goals) = intercept + home-advantage term + coefficient × Elo difference`. Each historical match contributes two data points (one for each team's goal count), exponentially down-weighted by recency (older matches count for less, with a roughly 2.4-year half-life under the current decay setting) so the fitted relationship reflects recent football, not 1925 football, while still using the full historical record for stability.

**Technical note:** this is the stage where a genuine, fully-documented bug was found and traced (see Section 10) — the *training* code's home-advantage indicator did not check the match's `neutral` flag the same way every *other* file in the pipeline (prediction, backtesting, rho-fitting) did, producing a real train/serve mismatch affecting roughly 26% of the historical panel.

### 5.3 Dixon-Coles low-score correction and Negative-Binomial overdispersion (`topics/match-winner-goals-totals/model/dixon_coles.py`, `topics/match-winner-goals-totals/model/fit_rho.py`, `topics/match-winner-goals-totals/model/fit_nb_dispersion.py`)

**Plain English:** the Poisson stage gives expected-goals numbers; this stage turns those into a full scoreline probability grid (P(0-0), P(1-0), P(2-1), …) and applies two corrections. First, the Dixon-Coles "tau" correction (Section 3.1) nudges the four low-scoring cells to fix independent-Poisson's known under-prediction of draws and 1-0/0-1 scorelines, governed by a single dependence parameter ρ. Second, because backtesting found real scorelines have fatter tails than pure Poisson predicts (the model was underconfident in the very-low-goals range and overconfident in the middle range), a Negative-Binomial variant of the same grid is fit with an extra overdispersion parameter α (α=0 reduces exactly to Poisson).

**Technical note:** ρ is fit by maximum-likelihood grid search over the historical panel (not asserted) — the current best fit is ρ≈−0.06 (against a −0.10 placeholder used before the fit was run), and the project re-fits ρ a second time using the Negative-Binomial marginals once α is estimated, since the optimal low-score correction shifts slightly when the underlying marginal distribution family changes. From the scoreline grid, every market category the project currently models analytically falls out as simple arithmetic: match result (sum cells where home>away, etc.), total-goals over/under, both-teams-to-score, and "team scores ≥N goals."

### 5.4 Ordered logit (`topics/match-winner-goals-totals/model/ordered_logit.py`)

**Plain English:** this is the direct implementation of Hvattum & Arntzen's (2010) approach (Section 3.2) — instead of predicting goal counts and deriving Home/Draw/Away probabilities afterward, this model is fit *directly* against the actual three-way outcome using a proportional-odds ordered logistic regression, with Elo difference and a home-advantage indicator as the only two covariates, plus two fitted "cutpoints" that carve a single underlying strength-difference scale into three ordered outcome regions (Away win < Draw < Home win). Because it's calibrated against the exact quantity it's used to predict, this model backtests meaningfully better-calibrated than the goals-based pipeline in the high-confidence range, per the project's own diagnostics (Section 7) — the goals-based pipeline has a documented, persistent gap in that range.

### 5.5 How the two pipelines relate

The two approaches are not redundant: the goals-based pipeline (Sections 5.2–5.3) is the only one capable of pricing goal-total, BTTS, and correct-score-style markets, since it produces a full scoreline distribution; the ordered-logit model (5.4) only covers the match-result market but does so with better outcome-level calibration. `topics/match-winner-goals-totals/model/predict.py` and `topics/match-winner-goals-totals/model/predict_ordered_logit.py` are the corresponding end-to-end CLI tools, and in practice the project uses both and cross-checks them against each other and against the market (Section 9) rather than picking one a priori.

### 5.6 Validation discipline — expanding-window, not random k-fold

All of the above is validated via a **time-ordered, walk-forward backtest** (`topics/match-winner-goals-totals/model/backtest_harness.py`, `topics/match-winner-goals-totals/model/backtest_diagnostics.py`, `topics/match-winner-goals-totals/model/backtest_single.py`), explicitly **not** random k-fold cross-validation — match outcomes are time-ordered, and the project's own coefficients are fit using only recency-weighted historical data, so any validation has to respect the same no-lookahead discipline the model itself is built to. `backtest_harness.py` reports mean Brier and mean Ranked Probability Score for the match-result market against the naive 33/33/33 baseline, plus a per-decile calibration table (predicted P(home win) vs. actual home-win rate). `backtest_single.py` is explicitly documented as "a sanity check / demo, not a statistical validation" — the project is careful to note that a single match's predicted-vs-actual comparison cannot validate or invalidate a probabilistic model on its own, since a well-calibrated 75%-confidence forecast is *correct* even in the 25% of instances where it's wrong. `backtest_diagnostics.py` runs two further discriminating checks (re-plotting the calibration gap in logit/log-odds space, and splitting by neutral-vs-non-neutral matches) specifically to distinguish between competing hypotheses about *why* a calibration gap exists, rather than just confirming that it does (see Section 10).

### 5.7 ML / calibration layer

`ml/build_feature_matrix.py` and `ml/platt_diagnostic.py` form a separate, lighter-weight ML layer applied not to the historical 49,000-match panel but to the project's own live JTC submission record — described fully in Section 7, since its purpose is calibration diagnosis of the live forecasting record rather than match-outcome modeling per se.

## 6. Crowd-Bias Model and Rules

### 6.1 The headline empirical finding

After enough settled questions accumulated to support a regression, the project fit a direct empirical relationship between its own submitted estimate and the platform's revealed crowd consensus (`project_crowd_bias_model.md`):

```
crowd − 0.5 ≈ 0.61 × (our_estimate − 0.5) + 0.014
```

(equivalently, `crowd ≈ 0.514 + 0.61×(our_estimate − 0.5)`), fit on n=85 questions across 9 matches, with a correlation of 0.83 and a residual standard deviation around 7 percentage points, later out-of-sample validated on a further match (BEL-EGY) with RMSE 5.8pp and essentially zero bias. In plain terms: whatever the project's own (evidence-grounded) estimate says, the crowd's number reliably comes in compressed by roughly 39% of the distance back toward 50%, plus a small uniform +1.4-percentage-point upward nudge. Splitting by direction sharpens this further: when the project's own estimate is below 0.5 (72% of all questions), its average estimate (0.361) lands almost exactly on the realized outcome base rate (0.361) — i.e. close to perfectly calibrated in aggregate — while the crowd's average (0.430) overestimates how often "unlikely" events happen by about 7 percentage points; the mirror pattern holds (smaller, about 4pp) when the project's estimate is above 0.5.

The proposed mechanism, explicit in the project's own framing and consistent with the favorite-longshot misperception literature reviewed in Section 3.4 (Snowberg & Wolfers 2010): JTC's crowd is the average of free, Brier-scored guesses with no liquidity discipline (unlike a real-money exchange), so individual participants rarely commit to genuinely extreme probabilities, and the aggregate regresses toward 50/50 regardless of where the truth actually sits. This is treated explicitly as a **live, periodically-revalidated empirical parameter** — directly analogous to how clubelo.com's published methodology frames its home-field-advantage term as a self-calibrating, continuously-checked-against-outcomes parameter rather than a one-time fitted constant (Section 1 of the structural research behind this document) — not as a fixed law assumed to hold forever.

### 6.2 An important honesty check on the model itself

The crowd-bias memo is explicit that, taken in isolation, the crowd's *raw* Brier score across the same 85-question sample (0.2268) is actually *better* than the project's own raw Brier score (0.2424) — the project wins only 52.9% of individual questions head-to-head. The large positive cumulative RBP comes from winning disproportionately on *higher-leverage* questions (where the platform's scoring weights bigger gaps more), not from being right more often in a simple count sense; and the project's four worst-ever individual results at the time of that analysis (a combined −153.85 RBP) shared a single root cause — confident "Team A more X than Team B" comparison props with no market to cross-check, where a historical gap looked clean but did not transfer to the new fixture. This same memo notes that removing just the single worst result (a gut-override loss, see Section 8) would have raised total RBP by 37% — i.e., the strategy is **positive-expected-value but genuinely high-variance**, concentrated in one identifiable question family, not a uniformly safe edge.

### 6.3 Named situational rules (selected, evidence-graded)

On top of the baseline crowd-compression formula, more than a dozen named rules (RULE1 through RULE15, plus several unconfirmed "watch items") encode situational corrections discovered by tracing specific match outcomes. A representative selection, each with its evidence status as of the most recent record:

- **RULE1 (match-winner questions):** estimate = average(crowd consensus, verified sharp market). Backtested across the first 3 settled match-winner questions, this would never have been the single worst of {crowd, market, average} available — a minimax-regret rule, not an accuracy claim, given the small sample it was built on.
- **RULE7 ("favorite's underdog-prop edge"):** when a team is priced above ~90% to win, the project's edge concentrates in the *props* about the underdog, not the headline match-winner pick — confirmed n≥5, the basis of the campaign's best-ever single match (Germany 7-1 Curaçao, Section 8).
- **RULE8 (high-draw-price shrinkage):** when the pre-match draw probability is elevated (≳25%), shrink "Team A more [count stat] than Team B" estimates further toward 50% than the raw historical gap suggests, because evenly-matched teams' counting stats converge (and ties resolve "no"). Confirmed at n=3 with a clean record (MEX-KOR, JPN-NED, CIV-ECU); later refined to a milder 0.55–0.60 lean rather than a hard 0.50 assumption after a counterexample (CAN-BIH, a 1-1 draw with a 10-foul differential) showed the shrinkage could itself overcorrect.
- **RULE12 (pre-submission risk gate):** before submitting a no-market "X more/fewer than Y" comparison prop, check whether removing the single most extreme game from either team's small sample changes the estimate by more than 10 percentage points; if so, treat the estimate as low-confidence and pull it toward what the crowd-formula would predict for an equivalent *high*-confidence estimate, rather than leaving it at its full distance from 50%.
- **RULE15 (extreme-underdog forward suppression):** confirmed across multiple players (n≥5: Fajardo, Sučić, Džeko, Afif, Son) that a team priced under ~15–20% to win should have its primary attacker's shots-on-target/scoring probability suppressed into roughly a 0.20–0.35 band regardless of club-level rate — but this rule required a **major, evidence-forced revision** after it failed badly (−31.86 RBP) on Sabitzer in ARG-AUT: the original suppressing evidence (Sabitzer's 0 SOT in a prior match) came from when his team was *comfortably dominant*, not from when they were *trailing and chasing* — exactly the opposite tactical role, which instead tends to *increase* a captain's shot volume. The rule now explicitly branches on "blowout underdog" (suppress) vs. "competitive underdog chasing a 1-goal deficit" (do not suppress, may even invert).
- **2+ offsides "family":** the single most repeated rule in the whole campaign — defensive/deep-block underdogs are reliably priced too high by the crowd on "2+ offsides" props — but it underwent its own evidence-forced revision after two consecutive failures (Haiti down 0-3, Turkey chasing 0-1) showed the rule **inverts completely** once a team is actually behind on the scoreboard and pushing players forward in desperation, regardless of its pre-match defensive profile.

The pattern across nearly every rule above — initial confirmation, then a concrete, named failure that forces a more conditional, context-sensitive version — is itself a structural feature of how this project has been built: rules are evidence-graded and revised in public, not asserted once and left static.

## 7. Calibration and Validation

### 7.1 Historical-panel calibration (the 49,000-match backtest)

The walk-forward backtest of the goals-based pipeline against ~49,000 historical international matches (Section 5.6) surfaced a **persistent, roughly uniform 5–8 percentage point compression gap**: at every predicted P(home win) decile from 0.1 to 0.9, the actual home-win rate runs higher than predicted, by a broadly similar margin throughout the range. Re-fitting the Dixon-Coles ρ parameter (−0.10 placeholder → −0.06 MLE-fitted) barely moved this gap, which ruled out the low-score correction as the primary cause. `data/calibration_research_notes.md` treats this as an open diagnostic question rather than a solved one: it surveys the classical errors-in-variables / attenuation-bias literature (a noisy, partly-bootstrapped Elo covariate biasing the fitted Elo-difference coefficient toward zero, which mechanically compresses predicted probabilities toward 50/50) as the leading hypothesis, cross-references Glicko/TrueSkill's explicit handling of *rating uncertainty* as a structural alternative to a point-estimate Elo, and recommends — as the single highest-value, lowest-cost next diagnostic step — re-plotting the same calibration gap in logit (log-odds) space and separately for neutral vs. non-neutral matches, specifically because that single re-plot discriminates between the attenuation-bias hypothesis and a home-field-advantage-miscalibration hypothesis. [NEEDS VERIFICATION: whether this logit-space/neutral-split diagnostic has actually been run and what it found — `calibration_research_notes.md` frames it as the recommended next step, and the related code-audit (Section 10) addresses a different, related bug rather than this specific diagnostic.]

### 7.2 Live-record calibration (the Platt-scaling diagnostic)

Separately from the historical-panel work, `ml/platt_diagnostic.py` runs a calibration check on the project's own **live JTC submission record** (246 settled questions at the time of that run, 2026-06-11 to 2026-06-22 — see Section 4c's note on dataset vintage): a two-parameter Platt-scaling logistic fit of `logit(P(outcome=1)) = a + b × logit(our_estimate)`. The global fit gives `a = −0.21`, `b = 0.51` — nominally suggesting both a slight tendency to submit too high and meaningful overconfidence (estimates too extreme) — but the bootstrap 95% confidence interval for `b` is `[0.12, 1.29]`, which **comfortably includes 1.0** (perfect calibration); at this sample size, the data cannot yet statistically distinguish "genuinely overconfident" from "noise." Critically, a walk-forward check (fit on the first 60% chronologically, 147 questions, apply to the remaining 40%, 99 questions) shows the fitted slope is wildly unstable across the split (`b=0.51` globally vs. `b=0.196` walk-forward) and that **applying the Platt correction actually worsens out-of-sample Brier score**, by 0.028 — a clear, explicitly-documented decision **not** to apply any calibration correction yet, on the grounds that doing so would be fitting noise rather than signal at the current sample size. The project's own stated next checkpoint is to revisit this once the live record reaches roughly 350 questions.

One genuine structural finding does survive this caution, however: questions where the project's submitted estimate falls in the 70–100% ("HIGH confidence") range earn a mean RBP of only +0.65, versus +2.7 to +3.1 in every other estimate range, despite a comparable beat-crowd rate — consistent with the favorite-longshot literature's prediction that when both the model and the crowd are already confident about a likely favorite, there is little remaining gap to exploit.

### 7.3 The reconciliation between the two calibration checks

These two diagnostics are not in conflict, but they answer different questions at different scales: Section 7.1 audits the *historical-panel statistical model* (the Elo/Poisson/Dixon-Coles machinery) against 49,000 matches it was never specifically tuned to win individual head-to-head bets on; Section 7.2 audits the *actual submitted JTC estimates* (which blend the statistical model with the manual rules-based research workflow of Section 6) against the live tournament record the project is actually scored on. The historical-panel gap is large, persistent, and not yet fully explained; the live-record gap is smaller, statistically inconclusive at current sample size, and explicitly not yet acted upon. Both are presented in this writeup precisely because the recommended structure for this kind of writeup treats negative or inconclusive calibration findings as worth reporting plainly, not smoothing over.

### 8.3 The worst result on record, without spin

**Brazil 1–1 Morocco** (2026-06-14), specifically the "Will Brazil win the match?" question: −51.97 RBP, the single worst individual result in the project's history. The researched recommendation — RULE1's average of the market price (57.87%) and a predicted crowd-inflation premium — was **0.58–0.60**. The actual submission was **1.00**, a personal-conviction override with no researched basis. Brazil drew. Under squared-error scoring, submitting 1.00 and being wrong costs the maximum possible penalty (squared error of exactly 1.0); submitting the researched 0.60 would have scored 0.36 — which would itself have beaten the crowd's own (too-high) estimate of 0.67 (squared error 0.4489). This single postmortem produced a durable rule (discount or zero out the home-advantage term specifically for tournament-host matches) and seeded the project's later, more general practice of treating a >10-percentage-point divergence between the model and a multi-source verified market as a signal to shade heavily toward the market rather than leave the divergence "flagged but uninvestigated."

## 9. Market-Edge / Backtest Pipeline

### 9.1 What "edge" means operationally in this project

Two distinct comparisons run in parallel throughout the project, and it is worth being precise about which is which: (1) **model vs. real betting market**, an absolute, statistically-testable backtest against bookmaker/exchange odds (this section); and (2) **submitted estimate vs. JTC crowd consensus**, the relative, RBP-scored comparison that the tournament itself is built on (Sections 6–8). The two are related — a market-beating model is a *candidate* input to a crowd-beating submission — but they are not the same exercise, and the project keeps them in separate scripts.

### 9.2 Model-vs-market backtest (`topics/match-winner-goals-totals/model/backtest_vs_market.py`)

This script backtests the ordered-logit match-result model against real historical bookmaker odds (`data/external/odds/international_fixture_odds.csv`) — used here as the closest available large-sample proxy for "the crowd," since the JTC platform itself does not expose a historical, machine-readable record of its own past crowd consensus values. For every fixture appearing in both the odds file and the historical Elo panel with a final score, it de-vigs the bookmaker odds (`p_j = (1/odds_j) / Σ(1/odds_k)`, the standard normalization to remove the bookmaker's built-in margin), computes both model and market three-way Brier and RPS scores, and reports a head-to-head win rate with a formal sign test (binomial test) and a paired t-test on the Brier-score differential, plus a breakdown by market-implied favorite strength (toss-up / lean / moderate favorite / heavy favorite) specifically to check for the kind of favorite-longshot-bias-style edge documented in the literature review. [NEEDS VERIFICATION: this writeup read the script in full but did not find a saved output/results file from an actual run of `backtest_vs_market.py` — the reported sign-test win rate, p-value, and bucket-level breakdown are not available to quote here and should be filled in once that script has been run and its output saved.]

### 9.3 Live prop-market pricing (`bot/predict_markets.py`, `data/prop_market_pricing_notes.md`)

For the JTC props the analytical Poisson/ordered-logit pipeline does not natively cover (fouls, cards, corners, offsides, individual player props — flagged "no_model" by `bot/classify_markets.py`, Section 4b), the project's general framework (`data/prop_market_pricing_notes.md`) is to treat the existing Elo/Poisson backbone as a **source of covariates**, not a complete model: fit a small count regression (Poisson or Negative-Binomial, chosen by overdispersion) of the relevant event count on Elo difference plus context (home/neutral, confederation), then derive "team total ≥ N" probabilities from the fitted distribution's CDF, or "Team A more than Team B" probabilities via the **Skellam distribution** (the distribution of the difference of two Poisson random variables, with a closed-form or easily-summed CDF) for head-to-head comparison props. `bot/predict_markets.py` is the corresponding live script: it computes a suggested submission value for every market the project's models *can* currently price (match winner via ordered logit; goal-total and BTTS-style markets via the Negative-Binomial-corrected Dixon-Coles grid) and writes a review CSV for manual confirmation before anything is submitted to the platform — i.e., model output is never auto-submitted, it is always a recommendation a human checks first. `bot/fetch_smarkets_mapping.py` and `bot/team_code_map.py` (Section 4b) are the supporting infrastructure that make a live Smarkets market price available as a cross-check input to this process at all.

### 9.4 A concrete lesson in *not* naively pricing from illiquid markets

`STEPS_FOR_HIGH_POINTS.md`'s "Win Pattern #7" documents a clean, repeatable methodological point directly relevant to this section: for a Smarkets market structured as many individual illiquid player-level contracts (e.g., "will any player score 2+ goals," built from 44 separate per-player contracts), naively summing the offer-side prices across all 44 contracts produced a meaningless 0.76 — illiquid overpricing on thin individual contracts compounds across many of them rather than averaging out. The correct approach, used successfully (RSA-CAN, +11.94 RBP), was to back out team-level goal expectations from the *liquid* team-total market instead, and explicitly model the player-level split via a Poisson-binomial decomposition with an assumed top-scorer goal-share — arriving at 0.18, well below both the naive-sum trap (0.76) and the crowd's own number (0.25), and the best-calibrated figure on the board once the match settled. The general principle this established: **decompose from the nearest liquid market and model the split explicitly; never sum raw prices across many thin, correlated contracts covering the same underlying event.**

## 10. Limitations and Threats to Validity

This section is deliberately a first-class, independent section rather than folded into a closing paragraph, per the explicit structural guidance behind this writeup — several of the academic exemplars surveyed during this writeup's preparation notably omit a dedicated limitations section, and that omission was identified as a gap worth not repeating here.

### 10.1 A confirmed, traced model bug: the host-nation / home-advantage overrating issue

The CAN-BIH postmortem (Section 8.4) identified a real bug, not just a calibration nuance: the ordered-logit match-winner model's home-advantage coefficient (`b_home=0.377`) was applied at full strength to a World Cup host-nation match (Canada, playing in Toronto), producing a 75.35% win probability against a verified multi-source market consensus around 54%. Even setting `b_home` to zero for that specific match, the remaining Elo gap alone (249 points) still implied 67.7% — meaningfully above the market's 54% — suggesting the underlying Elo panel itself may also be underrating teams that advanced through a difficult qualifying playoff bracket (Bosnia-Herzegovina beat or drew through Italy and Wales to qualify), a separate, only partially understood issue. **Caution on this finding's evolution:** an earlier framing of this issue as a general "World Cup host nations get overrated" bias was later explicitly debunked by the project's own further research (`project_crowd_bias_model.md`) — a review of historical World Cup host performance back to 1982 found hosts perform almost exactly at expectation on average, with very high variance (Qatar 2022 the worst-ever host performance on record) — so the CAN-BIH finding is better understood as a specific instance of model/Elo miscalibration for that one match and possibly tournament-host advantage specifically (still genuinely small relative to bilateral home advantage, per the literature reviewed in `calibration_research_notes.md` §3.4), not as a validated general host-nation forecasting bias.

### 10.2 A second, independently confirmed pipeline bug: train/predict semantic mismatch on `neutral`

A separate, line-by-line code audit (`data/code_audit_findings.md`) found that `topics/match-winner-goals-totals/model/poisson_goals.py`'s *training* code set its home-advantage indicator based purely on which team was labeled `home_team` in the data, with **no reference to the match's `neutral` flag at all** — while five other files in the same pipeline (`predict.py`, `backtest_harness.py`, `backtest_diagnostics.py`, `backtest_single.py`, `fit_rho.py`) all correctly gate the same indicator on `neutral`. This means the model was *trained* on one definition of "home advantage applies" and *evaluated/predicted* on a stricter, different definition, for roughly 26.4% of the 49,400-match historical panel (every neutral-venue match). The audit traces the likely direction and magnitude of the resulting bias and recommends a specific one-line fix plus a re-fit. **[FIXED 2026-07-10: The neutral check has been confirmed applied, the coefficients have been re-fit and verified (`poisson_goals_coefs.json`: b0=0.10408, b1=0.23022, b2=0.00181), and a detailed explanatory comment block was added to `build_design_matrix()` in `poisson_goals.py`. Note on bias direction: the audit predicted the bug attenuated b1 downward, but the actual comparison (buggy b1=0.256 vs fixed b1=0.230) showed the opposite — neutral-venue "home" teams skew slightly toward higher-rated sides by convention, so including them was inflating b1, not suppressing it. The gap from the prior estimate of ~0.35 is a separate calibration question unrelated to this bug. The core finding — inconsistent neutral handling between training and inference — is confirmed and the fix is correct.]** The same audit separately documents (without calling it a bug) that the historical dataset's `home_team`/`away_team` labeling convention for neutral-venue matches is not governed by any single documented rule — it is empirically closer to alphabetical-by-tournament-convention than to a quality- or seeding-based assignment, with the practical effect that "home_team" in a neutral match is the better-rated side about 56% of the time, a real but modest, non-random skew worth knowing about when interpreting any "neutral-match" diagnostic.

### 10.3 The live-record calibration question remains genuinely open

As detailed in Section 7.2, the Platt-scaling diagnostic on the project's own live submission record could not, at n=246, statistically distinguish "genuinely overconfident" from "sampling noise," and a walk-forward check showed attempting a correction anyway would have made things worse. This is reported here as an honest limitation, not resolved: the project does not currently know with confidence whether its own live estimates are systematically miscalibrated, only that if they are, the effect is not yet large or stable enough to safely correct for.

### 10.4 Data-quality-first principle, and where it has and hasn't yet been fully applied

The project's stated foundational rule for any ML work — "model results are only as good as your data," audit before modeling, never force-fit a technique the data can't support — is documented as having driven the sequencing of the ML integration effort (data audit → feature-matrix build → Platt diagnostic, in that order, deliberately not skipping ahead to a more complex model). That same audit explicitly flags that more sophisticated ML (gradient-boosted trees, in particular) was judged premature given current sample size and overfitting risk, and that a flat, fully joined cross-match feature matrix linking pre-match features to question-level outcomes — the prerequisite for most further ML work — only exists in a partial form (Section 4c's note that pre-match research enrichment covers ~24% of rows). The principle has been consistently *stated* and used to justify *not* doing things (Section 7.2's explicit "do not apply Platt correction now" decision is a direct application of it); whether every part of the pipeline has been audited to the same standard the principle calls for is not fully verifiable from the files reviewed for this writeup.

### 10.5 Structural limitations of the forecasting setting itself

This is single-person research, not peer-reviewed, scored against an opaque crowd-consensus mechanism whose exact formula (the precise RBP scale constant, and whether it behaves more like Brier or RPS per Section 3.3's distinction) has not been independently confirmed against the live platform. The crowd-bias correction model (Section 6) and the situational rules built on top of it are fit on a relatively small, rapidly-accumulating sample (low hundreds of questions) and have already required multiple evidence-forced revisions (the 2+ offsides family, RULE15) within the project's own short history — a pattern that should be read as a feature of honest, ongoing calibration, but also as a signal that any single rule's current form should be treated as provisional, not settled science, especially the rules confirmed at only n=1 to n=3 ("watch items" rather than "rules" in the project's own terminology).

## 12. Appendix: Data Dictionary / Repro Notes

### 12.1 `master_question_dataset.csv` — summary data dictionary

Full detail lives in `datasets/MASTER_DATASET_DICTIONARY.md`; summarized here per the recommended structure's "data dictionary lives separately, methodology lives in prose elsewhere, cross-linked" pattern (modeled on FiveThirtyEight's soccer-spi README). Built by `datasets/build_master_dataset.py`, read-only against all raw data, writing only to this one CSV. **480 rows** (one per match-question pair), **73 columns**, drawn from **49 match files** under `data/external_markets/*.json`.

**Match-level columns (repeated across each match's ~10 rows):** `file`, `match` (canonical 3-letter-code pair, e.g. `ARG-AUT`), `team_a`/`team_b` (full names), `team_a_code`/`team_b_code`, `date`, `tournament`/`group`/`game_day`, `venue_raw`/`venue_city`/`venue_country`, `altitude_m`, `schema_era` (1/2/3, which generation of raw-file schema), `post_match_key_used`, `final_score`, `match_total_rbp`, `settled_date`, `match_submitted` (False only for URU-CPV), `match_is_calibration_only`, `elo_team_a_pre`/`elo_team_b_pre`/`elo_diff`, `rest_days_a`/`rest_days_b`/`rest_days_diff`, `squad_value_a_eur`/`squad_value_b_eur`, `squad_avg_age_a`/`squad_avg_age_b`, `fifa_ranking_2025_a`/`fifa_ranking_2025_b`, `draw_probability`, `poisson_lambda_a`/`poisson_lambda_b`, `rules_fired_list`/`rule_fired_count`, `rule1_fired` … `rule15_fired`.

**Question-level columns (one value per row):** `question_num` (post-match/platform numbering — see the Q-number caveat below), `question_text`, `question_category` (`tier1_market`, `tier2_realdata`, `stats_proxy`, `compound_market_proxy`, `skellam_market_hybrid`, `match_winner_deferred_market`, `match_winner_gut_override`), `confidence`/`direction`, `key_drivers`, `edge_rank`, `recommended_estimate` (pre-match research figure, matched by topic-word overlap — only ~24% coverage, a genuine data gap not a matching-threshold artifact), `our_estimate`, `submission_diff`/`submission_error_flag` (5 questions flagged across the whole dataset, magnitude > 0.03 — includes the documented ENG-CRO Kane transcription error), `crowd_estimate`, `outcome`/`outcome_known`, `rbp` (blank, not 0, for never-submitted questions — a deliberate distinction from the raw files' literal `0`), `beat_crowd`, `actually_submitted` (False for ~20 questions: 11 in BIH-QAT, never submitted before deadline; 10 in URU-CPV, calibration-only), `is_player_prop`/`player_key`, `postmortem_note`.

**Documented data-quality caveat (load-bearing, not cosmetic):** question numbers are **not** a reliable join key across different containers within the same raw file — verified concretely on `eng_cro_2026-06-17.json` (pre-match `q5` vs. post-match `Q7` for the same Kane SOT question) and `cro_pan_2026-06-23.json` (a rotation-by-one between draft and final numbering for the first four questions). The build script resolves this via topic-word tokenization, Jaccard similarity (≥0.35, requiring ≥2 shared tokens), and a global greedy best-match assignment — never by assuming positional consistency. The core scored fields (`our_estimate`, `crowd_estimate`, `outcome`, `rbp`) are **not** affected by this problem, since they all come from the post-match record's own internally-consistent numbering (independently spot-checked against 12 RBP figures documented elsewhere in the project, all matching exactly).
