### Q1 and Q8 (both Qatar-SOT, both big losses) - root cause confirmed

**Q1 (HT both teams 1+ SOT)**: us=0.20, crowd=0.59, outcome=YES, RBP=-42.51 (worst single
question across all 4 matches; next-worst was -14.53). Root cause: a CORRELATED-error
convergence (n=2 and n=5 Qatar 1H-SOT samples both ~0.20-0.24, treated as validation when both
used the same compound-independence Poisson framework on the same underlying low-1H-SOT
assumption). "Both teams get >=1 SOT by halftime" is a LOW BAR with a much higher real-world
base rate than a thin team-specific sample implies.

**Q8 (Qatar 2+ SOT full match)**: us=0.39, crowd=0.57, outcome=YES, RBP=-14.3. Our session-3
revision correctly computed the n=5 "vs strong opposition" raw Poisson estimate at 0.475 (sq
err 0.276, would have nearly halved this loss) - but then SHADED IT DOWN to 0.39 because of the
"Qatar scoreless 373 minutes, attacking crisis" UNVERIFIED_SEARCH_SUMMARY narrative. That shade
was in the WRONG DIRECTION.

**Were the sources at fault for Q1/Q8?** The VERIFIED real-data sources (ESPN n=5/n=12
boxscores) were accurate and, if anything, UNDER-TRUSTED - their raw output (0.475 for Q8) was
closer to truth than our final answer. The UNVERIFIED qualitative "attacking crisis" narrative
wasn't necessarily false as a general trend, but it's a weak predictor for a SINGLE match
outcome - and we used it to shade an already-low statistical estimate even lower.

### Q2 and Q6 (the two "creative dig" revisions) - BOTH made things worse

This is the most uncomfortable finding from the complete data. Both of session 3's headline
"creative digs" - the two LARGEST single-question swings of the whole match - moved us AWAY
from crowd and turned what would have been wins or near-wins into clear losses:

- **Q2 (Switzerland 2+ offsides)**: outcome=NO. Our session-3 revision (0.42 -> 0.60, the
  largest single revision in the match, driven by "opponent vs Qatar averages 2.5
  offsides/match, n=10") moved us further from 0. Squared error: us(0.60)=0.36 vs
  crowd(0.53)=0.281 -> below crowd, RBP=-3.84. Our PRE-revision number (0.42) would have scored
  0.176 - BETTER than crowd, likely a small POSITIVE RBP. The revision cost us roughly
  +5 to +8 RBP of swing.
- **Q6 (Qatar more fouls than Switzerland)**: outcome=YES (Qatar DID out-foul Switzerland).
  Our session-3 reversal (0.66 -> 0.46, based on "Switzerland's tournament foul rate (12.56) >
  Qatar's (11.5) once compared apples-to-apples") moved us from above-50% to below-50% -
  exactly the WRONG side of the eventual outcome. Squared error: us(0.46)=0.292 vs
  crowd(0.64)=0.130 -> below crowd, RBP=-13.81. Our PRE-reversal number (0.66) would have
  scored 0.116 - BETTER than crowd, likely a small POSITIVE RBP. This reversal alone is worth
  roughly +15 to +18 RBP of swing - nearly as damaging as Q8.

**Why this matters**: both digs were methodologically sound (real ESPN boxscore data, correct
arithmetic, defensible reasoning about context-matching). But both were built on small samples
(n=9, n=10) of OTHER teams' matches projected onto a single Qatar match, and both swings moved
us AWAY from where the crowd already was. In both cases the crowd's more central, less-revised
number ended up closer to this particular outcome. Combined, these two revisions cost roughly
**+20 to +26 RBP** relative to if we'd submitted the pre-session-3 numbers - on a day where the
total loss was -39.21, these two "improvements" account for over half the damage beyond Q1.

### Q3 - the best single result of the day (+19.86, our best-ever positive RBP)

**Q3 (Switzerland 1+ card 2H)**: us=0.40, crowd=0.57, outcome=NO, RBP=+19.86. Crowd was on the
WRONG side of 50% (57% for an event that didn't happen); we were correctly below 50%. This was
the ORIGINAL session-1/2 blend - averaging the live Smarkets full-match cards market (implying
~53% for 2H) with Switzerland's real recent-match card rate (~30% 2H) to land at 0.40. This
blend-of-market-and-real-data approach (NOT touched by session-3 revisions) is exactly the kind
of estimate that should be trusted MORE, not less.

### Q4, Q7, Q9, Q10 - tier1_market(_deviated) all held up fine

Q4 (+2.85) and Q9/Q10 (+7.26, +10.91) were straightforward tier1_market/tier1-ish estimates
that were already close to or better-positioned than crowd and needed no revision. Q7 (+2,
small win despite us=0.38 having a slightly larger squared error than crowd=0.40 - another
reminder the RBP formula isn't pure squared error) shows the small downward shade for the
"attacking crisis" narrative did NOT hurt here, even though the same narrative hurt Q8 - the
narrative wasn't "wrong," it was specifically mis-applied to Q8's already-low estimate.

### Overall lesson for future matches

1. **RULE 4 (Q1/Q8 pattern) holds**: don't shade an already-low "underdog offensive output"
   estimate down further with recency narratives; for compound "both teams achieve X" questions,
   weight toward crowd.
2. **NEW - be skeptical of LARGE late-session revisions from small-sample (n<10) "creative
   digs," especially when they move the estimate to the OPPOSITE side of 50% from where it was,
   or far from the crowd, without a verified market to anchor against.** Today, both of our two
   biggest revisions (Q2 +18pp, Q6 -20pp swing) were directionally wrong for this specific match
   and turned likely small wins into real losses. Treat "this new sample contradicts our prior
   estimate AND the crowd" as a flag to soften the revision (e.g. blend old and new rather than
   fully replacing), not as license to swing hard.
3. **Blending market-anchor with real-data-anchor (Q3's approach) continues to be the strongest
   single technique** - our best-ever positive RBP came from exactly this, untouched by
   late revisions.

### Q8 (Brazil win, -51.97) - new worst-ever, and entirely a discipline failure

The RESEARCHED recommendation for this question was 0.58-0.60 (RULE1: avg(Smarkets
match-winner 57.87%, and a predicted crowd-inflation premium for a global-favorite team). The
SUBMITTED value was 1.00 - a personal-conviction override, not a methodology output. Brazil
drew 1-1 (did not win), so outcome=0.

The damage from going to 100% is structural, not just "being wrong":
- At us=1.00: squared error = (0-1.00)^2 = **1.00** (the maximum possible)
- At us=0.60 (the actual recommendation): squared error = (0-0.60)^2 = 0.36
- Crowd's squared error: (0-0.67)^2 = 0.4489
- **Even RULE1's literal avg(crowd, market) = avg(0.67, 0.5787) = 0.624** gives squared error
  0.389 - still better than crowd.

In other words: **every single number we actually researched for this question - 0.58, 0.60,
or 0.624 - would have BEATEN crowd and turned this into a positive RBP.** Crowd's 0.67 was
itself inflated above the 57.87% market (exactly the "big team" bias the literature predicted,
and which our pre-match analysis explicitly called out as a risk) - but going to 100% means we
were not just wrong in the same direction as crowd, we were maximally wrong, with literally
zero possible additional upside if Brazil HAD won (100% vs 90% only saves 0.01 of squared
error; 100% vs 0% costs 1.00 if wrong vs ~0.81 at 90%).

### Q10 (HT tied, -9.52) - likely the same bias, smaller bill

Recommended 0.45 (Smarkets HT-result market, Draw normalized to 44.57%). Submitted 0.32, a
13pp deviation AWAY from both market and crowd (0.42). Outcome was YES (HT was tied 0-0).
Had the recommended 0.45 been submitted, squared error = (1-0.45)^2 = 0.3025 - better than
crowd's 0.3364, a small win instead of a -9.52 loss. The downward shift away from "tied" is
consistent with the same "Brazil will be ahead, dominant" mental model that produced the Q8
override - one underlying belief cost us on two separate questions.

### Overall lesson for future matches

1. **NEW - RULE 6: never submit 0% or 100% on any probability question, especially
   match-winner.** Going to an extreme caps the maximum possible benefit (saving ~0.01-0.04 of
   squared error vs. a strong-but-bounded 90-95%) while uncapping the maximum possible cost
   (a full 1.00 squared error if wrong, vs ~0.81-0.90 at 90-95%). This is true REGARDLESS of how
   confident you feel - probability estimation is about the distribution of outcomes, not your
   level of conviction.
2. **The methodology had its best day yet.** 9/10 questions followed the researched
   process (first-pass tier2_realdata models, or tier1_market with minor crowd-tracking drift)
   and produced +35.65 RBP at a 7/10 beat-crowd rate - the strongest single-match validation of
   the Phase-3 retrospective so far.
3. **The crowd-inflation prediction was correct.** Crowd ran above the Smarkets market on
   the match-winner (0.67 vs 0.5787) AND on most of Brazil's props (Q4, Q6, Q7, Q9 all saw
   crowd >= our market-anchored recommendation) - exactly the "big team" bias flagged in
   [[project_crowd_bias_model]]. The mistake was not in identifying this bias, but in our own
   gut estimate being driven by the SAME bias, amplified past even what crowd was willing to
   commit to.

probability is elevated (here 27.9%, signaling a closely-matched contest), shrink the
historical-average-implied estimate further toward/below 0.50 than the raw Skellam gap
suggests - because ties become more likely as team quality converges, and "more than" always

### Q2 (-33.9, our 3rd-worst-ever) - NEW RULE10: blowouts invert the "underdog fouls more" prior

Pre-match, ESPN gave a strong, consistent signal: Sweden's last 5 matches averaged 8.6 fouls
(range 6-15), Tunisia's averaged 15.4 (range 9-29) - the ranges barely overlapped. Raw
Skellam said P(Sweden>Tunisia fouls)=0.08; we shrunk to 0.15 for RULE8 general caution. Crowd
said 0.40.

**Actual: Sweden 10 fouls, Tunisia 8 fouls - Sweden fouled MORE.** Breaking down where the
miss came from:
- Sweden's 10 fouls ≈ their own average (8.6) - Sweden played close to "normal."
- Tunisia's 8 fouls is WAY below their average (15.4) - more than HALVED relative to their
  typical rate.

The miss is almost entirely on Tunisia's side. Sweden won 5-1; Tunisia, on the wrong end of a
blowout, fouled far less than their history suggested - the same "blowout fatigue"/
disengagement mechanism documented for Curacao in GER-CUR (who went 19 minutes without a foul
after falling to 5-1 down, and finished with 11 fouls vs Germany's 18).

**This produces a clean, mechanistic, 2/2 pattern - NEW RULE10**: in matches that become
BLOWOUTS, the WINNING team commits MORE (or equal) fouls than the LOSING team, REGARDLESS of
either team's historical average:
- GER-CUR: Germany (winner) 18 fouls > Curacao (loser) 11 fouls. Germany's own average was
  HIGHER than Curacao's, so this matched our (correctly unshaded) estimate - we won +23.36.
- SWE-TUN: Sweden (winner) 10 fouls > Tunisia (loser) 8 fouls. Sweden's own average was LOWER
  than Tunisia's, so this CONTRADICTED our estimate - we lost -33.9.

The likely mechanism: the dominant team has more of the ball in advanced/pressing positions
(more opportunities and motivation to commit tactical fouls while attacking or pressing high
to win the ball back), while the team being blown out disengages - less pressing, less
urgency, fewer fouls, once the result feels decided.

**The cruel twist**: Q1 (Tunisia more corners than Sweden at HT) had the EXACT SAME underlying
pattern - Tunisia's corner average (3.8) also failed to transfer (full-match corners ended
Sweden 4, Tunisia 2) - but Q1 WON (+6.06) because a verified Smarkets corner-handicap market
existed (Sweden favored 61.5% to win the corner battle) and we deferred to it INSTEAD OF
Tunisia's ESPN average. Q2 had no equivalent fouls market - we had nothing to override the
misleading historical average with.

### How to apply RULE10 going forward

"Team A fouls more than Team B" (or any count-stat comparison with no market) is now
confirmed HIGH VARIANCE: per-team historical averages reflect each team's RELATIVE DOMINANCE
in THEIR OWN recent sample (a team that's been dominating weaker opposition racks up fouls via
pressing; a team that's been outclassed racks up fewer), which does NOT transfer cleanly to a
NEW opponent where the dominance relationship may be different or reversed. Two concrete
checks before trusting a historical-average gap on a fouls/corners "more than" prop:
1. **Is there ANY related market** (corner handicap, cards handicap, even a goal-margin/
   handicap market) that reflects the market's view of WHO WILL DOMINATE this specific
   matchup? If yes, weight it heavily - it already prices in the relevant "who presses more"
   dynamic that team-level historical averages can't capture.
2. **If no market exists**, treat the historical-average gap as a weak prior and shrink hard
   toward 50% - especially if the gap is driven by ONE team's average being unusually
   high/low (as Tunisia's 15.4, inflated by a 29-foul outlier vs Mali, was here). A
   consistent-LOOKING signal (narrow, non-overlapping ranges) is not the same as a
   TRANSFERABLE signal.
